    T.S. Eliot (1888–1965)¶

    Life and Works¶

    T.S. Eliot was the leading poet of Modernism in the 1910s and 1920s. In 1922 he published The Waste Land, a long poem with quotations in six languages and adaptations from Dante, Baudelaire and many other writers, following a collage technique typical of Modernism. Eliot was born in the US but lived most of his life in England. He was a great experimenter but also a classicist. He started with a deeply pessimistic view of life, which later turned into Christian acceptance. He received the Nobel Prize for literature in 1948.

    Thomas Stearns Eliot was born in 1888 into a rich family of St Louis, Missouri. The Eliots descended from New England Puritans, and a strong sense of civic and religious duty was part of the family tradition. In 1906 he went to Harvard, where he studied Renaissance literature, philosophy, Hindu and Buddhist philosophy, and Sanskrit — all of which later entered The Waste Land. He graduated with a dissertation on the philosopher F.H. Bradley, whose ideas on the psychological nature of experience influenced his poetry. After Harvard he went to the Sorbonne in Paris (1910), where he studied the philosophy of Henri Bergson and began to read the French Symbolist poets. He then travelled to Germany and, at the beginning of World War I in 1914, moved to Merton College, Oxford.

    1915 was a crucial year. Eliot moved to London and went to work at Lloyd's Bank, a job he held until 1925 but never liked. He also married Vivienne Haigh-Wood, a talented woman afflicted by bad health and bad nerves. He had begun to write but was not happy with the poetry of the time, which seemed imprecise and out of touch with the modern world. The meeting with Ezra Pound and the poetry of Imagism, with its insistence on clear and precise images, was decisive. Through Pound, the magazine Poetry published Eliot's first major poem, The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock (1915), followed by Poems (1919). These early poems deal with the emptiness of modern upper-class life and owe much to the French Symbolists.

    The Nihilistic Phase¶

    The years 1915–25 were very hard for Eliot. Money problems, working full-time in a bank, poor relations with his wife, and a general dissatisfaction with modern life brought him to a nervous breakdown in November 1921. He went to rest first in Margate (southern England) and then in a Swiss sanatorium in Lausanne. During this time he finished the first draft of The Waste Land. On his way back to London he stopped in Paris to give the manuscript to Ezra Pound, who made extensive cuts and helped shape the final version. The Waste Land came out in 1922, making Eliot the leading modernist poet in English. He left the bank and in 1925 became a director of the publishing house Faber and Gwyer (now Faber and Faber). The central metaphor of this nihilistic phase — which also includes Gerontion (1919) and The Hollow Men (1925) — is one of sterility, aridity, impotence.

    The Christian Phase and Late Years¶

    In the late 1920s Eliot found a way out of nihilism in religion. He was received into the Anglican Church in 1927, the year he also became a British subject. This is reflected in The Journey of the Magi (1927) and Ash Wednesday (1930), poems of quiet and hopeful searching for a higher truth. His play Murder in the Cathedral (1935), based on the martyrdom of Thomas Becket, and Four Quartets (1943) — four related poems written during World War II — represent the culmination of this phase and are perhaps his most personal works.

    In 1948 he received the Nobel Prize and the British Order of Merit. His wife Vivienne had died the previous year (he had separated from her in 1933, and in 1937 she had been committed to a mental institution). After the war Eliot was mostly occupied with drama, combining religious themes with contemporary subjects in plays like The Cocktail Party (1949), The Confidential Clerk (1954) and The Elder Statesman (1959). He married again in 1957 and died in London in 1965.

    Eliot's Modernism¶

    Eliot and Ezra Pound were the first great modernist poets of the English language. To the "waste land" of the modern world, Eliot opposed Western culture — the Middle Ages, the Renaissance, Indian philosophy, French Symbolism, modern music including jazz, and the Christian religion. His poetry is difficult, and he himself believed that in our time art should be difficult. The poems of the nihilistic phase are difficult because he removes connective passages and puts disparate images side by side. The poems of the Christian phase are more traditional in structure, but their philosophical background is complex.

    Poetical Sources¶

    From the Imagists (Ezra Pound) Eliot learned the necessity for clear, precise language and economy of words. From the French Symbolists (especially Baudelaire) he took free verse and the idea that the ugliness of modern city life could be a subject for poetry. He was deeply influenced by John Donne and the Metaphysical poets, admiring their wit and daring images. Perhaps the single greatest influence was Dante: Eliot found in the Divina Commedia the expression not of an individual but of a whole civilisation, and a complete range of emotion from hell's despair to heaven's beatitude.

    Two important critical concepts come from Eliot's essays. The first is the "objective correlative": the idea that a poet should not express emotions directly, but should find "a set of objects, a situation, a chain of events" that will produce the same emotion in the reader — so that "when the external facts are given, the emotion is immediately evoked." The second is the "dissociation of sensibility": according to Eliot, modern poetry had lost the unity of intellect and emotion that the Metaphysical poets still had — they were able to "feel their thought as immediately as the odour of a rose."

    Literary and Social Criticism¶

    Eliot was also a very important literary critic. In Tradition and the Individual Talent (1919) he wrote that a writer should have "a perception, not only of the pastness of the past, but of its presence" — the whole of European literature from Homer onwards has a simultaneous existence. In For Lancelot Andrewes (1928) he described himself as "classicist in literature, royalist in politics, and Anglo-Catholic in religion." His social essays, like The Idea of a Christian Society (1939), raise fundamental questions about modern society.

    The Waste Land (1922)¶

    The Waste Land is perhaps the central work in modernist literature. The poem is divided into five sections: "The Burial of the Dead" (lines 1–76), "A Game of Chess" (lines 77–172), "The Fire Sermon" (lines 173–311), "Death by Water" (lines 312–21), and "What the Thunder Said" (lines 322–433).

    Cultural and Spiritual Sterility¶

    The poem is considered by many the most important poem of the 20th century. It expresses the modern artist's disillusion with the world and the desperate need for a new tradition. Eliot sees only ruin and desolation, and is concerned with the decay of Western civilisation after World War I. The central metaphor is the spiritual dryness and sterility of modern life — the death of Western culture through a lack of any belief, religious or other, that can give meaning to everyday existence. The poem cannot be reconstructed into a logical narrative: images are juxtaposed, not ordered. This fragmentation reflects the fragmentation of contemporary culture.

    The Mythical Structure¶

    The Waste Land brings together images of modern decadence with echoes and quotations from ancient myths. Eliot referred his readers to Sir James Frazer's The Golden Bough (1890), a study of pagan myths and fertility rites, and to Jessie L. Weston's From Ritual to Romance (1920), which relates ancient fertility myths to the medieval romances of the Holy Grail. In these romances we see a wasteland whose ruler, the Fisher King, has brought sterility to the land. The order of myth is projected on to the chaos of modern life.

    Eliot's Method¶

    The five unequal sections show no logical continuity. Eliot uses quotations from thirty-five writers in six languages, as well as allusions to philosophical and religious traditions. For example, the crowds flowing over London Bridge at the end of "The Burial of the Dead" are modelled on the crowds of damned souls in Dante's Inferno. The metrical pattern varies: lines change in length and rhythm in a form of free verse, although there are some regularities and rhymes.
