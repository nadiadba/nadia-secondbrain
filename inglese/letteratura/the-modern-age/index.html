    Modernism¶

    A New World¶

    After the Victorian Age, English society changed very quickly. In the 19th century religious faith had been questioned; in the early 20th century people found it difficult to believe in anything at all — religion, philosophy, art, science or progress. Three great changes shook the way people thought about reality. In 1905 Albert Einstein published the Theory of Relativity, and time and space were no longer absolute: reality could no longer be described as objective. Friedrich Nietzsche declared that traditional values, such as "the good", "the true" and "the beautiful", were decadent, and his ideas deeply influenced British writers. Sigmund Freud developed psychoanalysis and showed that the human mind has many hidden layers, the unconscious, where universal patterns guide behaviour. As a result, modernist writers no longer trusted the outside world: they turned inwards, towards the mind and the flow of thought.

    What is Modernism?¶

    The term Modernism describes the literature and art of the early 20th century that reacted against 19th-century conventions in completely new forms. Modernist writers broke down traditional literary genres, fragmented time and place, abandoned the linear plot, and used a complex language that often defied syntax, grammar and punctuation. They focused on psychological truth rather than realistic details, freely incorporated ancient and oriental myths, and preferred free verse to traditional metres. Although they were influenced by avant-garde movements like Cubism, Futurism, Dadaism and Surrealism, they did not reject the past: on the contrary, they tried to rescue what was valuable from tradition and use it to read the modern world.

    Critics usually divide Modernists into two generations. The first worked between 1922 and 1925, the years of the great masterpieces — Eliot's The Waste Land, Joyce's Ulysses and Woolf's Mrs Dalloway — and explored the unconscious in daily life. The second generation came in the 1930s, led by W.H. Auden, and dealt with social and political problems in a less obscure language. Some of them, like George Orwell, even went to fight in the Spanish Civil War against the fascist forces of General Franco.

    Modern Poetry¶

    Between 1900 and 1918 English poetry changed completely, especially thanks to two movements started by Ezra Pound: Imagism, which fixed clear thoughts in clear images, and Vorticism, which celebrated energy and dynamism. In Ireland, W.B. Yeats led the Celtic Revival and wrote Easter 1916, a poem that commemorates the Irish revolt against British rule. The central modernist poem, however, is The Waste Land (1922) by T.S. Eliot, built on the myth of the Holy Grail and on the idea that Western civilisation has become sterile and chaotic. Its style is typically modernist: no clear narrative order, juxtaposition of past and present, time shifts, stream of consciousness and many quotations in different languages. In the 1930s Auden's Refugee Blues dealt with the Jewish people fleeing Nazi Germany, and in the 1940s the New Apocalypse poets, inspired by the Welsh poet Dylan Thomas, returned to nature and emotion, becoming "new Romantics".

    Modern Novel¶

    The English novel changed in two stages. Between 1880 and 1920 the transitional novelists experimented with flashbacks, time-shifts and new ways of describing psychology. Joseph Conrad, in Heart of Darkness (1902), used an exotic setting to explore the dark side of man and to criticise European colonialism. D.H. Lawrence was the first to feel the impact of psychoanalysis: his Sons and Lovers (1913) describes an Oedipal complex against the background of provincial life. In the late 1910s and early 1920s the modern novel proper emerged with Joyce and Woolf, who reflected the loss of faith in tradition and the trauma of World War I. The greatest change was the breakdown of the division of time: past, present and future became a continuous flux, and the technical solution was the stream of consciousness, also called interior monologue. Later, E.M. Forster explored colonial themes in A Passage to India, and George Orwell created the dystopian novel with Nineteen Eighty-Four (1949), where Big Brother became the symbol of modern man's enslavement to mass media.

    Joyce and Eliot¶

    Joyce and Eliot are the two greatest modernists in English literature, and they share more than they seem. Both used the mythical method: Joyce based Ulysses on Homer's Odyssey, while Eliot built The Waste Land on the legend of the Holy Grail. Both fragmented their texts, juxtaposing past and present and using the stream of consciousness or a similar collage of voices to show the chaos of modern life. Both also focused on a great city, Dublin for Joyce and London for Eliot, presented as places of paralysis and sterility. The differences are equally important. Joyce wrote prose and chose an ordinary man, Leopold Bloom, as his hero, celebrating with affectionate irony the small details of everyday life. Eliot wrote poetry and described modern civilisation as a dying wasteland, with a more pessimistic tone and a constant search for spiritual meaning that would later lead him to Christianity. Where Joyce remains rooted in the body, in sex and in the comic, Eliot is intellectual, religious and tragic.

    Joyce and Woolf¶

    Joyce and Woolf are the two great novelists of Modernism, and both used the stream of consciousness to describe how a single day can contain a whole life — Bloomsday in Ulysses, Clarissa's day in Mrs Dalloway. Both rejected the omniscient narrator and placed the reader directly inside the mind of their characters. They differ, however, in style and atmosphere. Joyce's stream of consciousness is rough, ironic, almost chaotic, and reaches its extreme in Molly Bloom's monologue without punctuation. Woolf's stream is softer and more poetic: in her essay Modern Fiction she explained that what matters is not "time on the clock" but "time in the mind". Joyce uses the mythical method openly, while Woolf prefers psychological subtlety and is especially interested in female consciousness, as in To the Lighthouse.

    Woolf and Eliot¶

    Woolf and Eliot belonged to the same London circle and both explored inner time and the crisis of values. Eliot, however, expressed this crisis through poetry, ancient myths and images of sterility, while Woolf used the novel to follow the delicate flow of personal consciousness, often from a female point of view.
