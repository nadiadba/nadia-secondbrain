# Modernism

## A New Era

After the Victorian Age, English society changed quickly. In the 19th century religious faith had been questioned; in the early 20th century people found it difficult to believe in anything at all — religion, philosophy, art, science or progress. This sense of crisis is the background of Modernism.

Three great changes shook the way people thought about reality:

- In 1905 Albert Einstein published the Theory of Relativity. Time and space were no longer absolute, and reality could no longer be seen as objective.
- Friedrich Nietzsche declared that traditional values — "the good", "the true", "the beautiful" — were decadent. His ideas deeply influenced British writers.
- Sigmund Freud developed psychoanalysis. He showed that the mind has many hidden layers — the unconscious — and that human behaviour follows universal patterns.

!!! note "Why this matters"
    Modernist writers no longer trusted the outside world. They turned inwards, towards the mind, the unconscious and the flow of thought.

## What is Modernism?

The term Modernism describes the literature and art of the early 20th century that reacted against 19th-century ideas in completely new forms. Its main features are:

- the breakdown of traditional literary genres
- the fragmentation of time and place
- the collapse of the traditional plot, with no clear beginning or ending
- complex language that often defies syntax, grammar and punctuation
- the focus on psychological truth instead of realistic details
- an eclectic use of myth, including oriental myths and traditions
- the use of free verse instead of traditional metres

The first Modernists were influenced by the iconoclastic movements of the early 20th century (Cubism, Futurism, Dadaism, Surrealism), but unlike them they did not reject the past. They tried to rescue what was valuable from tradition and use it to interpret the modern world.

## The Two Generations

The first generation worked between 1922 and 1925, the years of the great masterpieces: Eliot's The Waste Land, Joyce's Ulysses, and Woolf's Mrs Dalloway. They focused on the unconscious in daily life and on how to describe it.

The second generation came in the 1930s. They were known as the "Oxford poets" or "progressive poets", led by W.H. Auden. They preferred more traditional forms and a less obscure language, and dealt with social and political problems. Some, like George Orwell, went to fight in the Spanish Civil War against the fascist forces of General Franco.

## Modern Poetry

Between 1900 and 1918 English poetry changed completely. The 1910s saw the first works of Ezra Pound and T.S. Eliot, and the shocking new poetry of World War I.

### Imagism, Vorticism, Celtic Revival

Two movements were very important for Modernism, both started by Ezra Pound:

- Imagism, which insisted on fixing clear thoughts in simple, clear images
- Vorticism, which celebrated energy, speed and dynamism

In Ireland, W.B. Yeats led the Celtic Revival. He helped revive the Irish theatre at the Abbey Theatre in Dublin. After meeting the French Symbolists and Pound, his poetry became more precise. His poem Easter 1916 commemorates the Easter Rising, the Irish revolt against British rule.

### Poetry and Myth

The central modernist poem is The Waste Land (1922) by T.S. Eliot. It is built on myth and anthropology, especially on the myth of the Holy Grail, and presents the failure of Western civilisation through images of sterility and chaos. Its features are typically modernist:

- no clear narrative order
- juxtaposition of past and present (also used by Joyce in Ulysses)
- time shifts and stream of consciousness
- many quotations from other writers, in different languages

### Second-generation Poets and the New Romanticism

In the 1930s W.H. Auden became the leader of the new generation. His poem Refugee Blues (1939) deals with the Jewish people fleeing from Nazi Germany. Later he turned to themes of solitude in an indifferent world (Musée des Beaux Arts, 1940).

In the 1940s the New Apocalypse poets took inspiration from the Book of the Apocalypse and from D.H. Lawrence. Their model was the Welsh poet Dylan Thomas, who loved nature and childhood and is therefore called a "new Romantic".

## Modern Novel

The English novel changed in two stages: the transitional novelists (1880–1920), then the modernist novelists.

### The Transitional Novelists

These writers experimented with new structures: flashbacks, time-shifts, different points of view, and new ways of describing psychology.

- Joseph Conrad, Polish but writing in English. His Heart of Darkness (1902) uses an exotic setting to explore the dark side of man and to criticise European colonialism in Africa.
- D.H. Lawrence, the first English novelist to feel the impact of psychoanalysis. His Sons and Lovers (1913) is both a psychological description of an Oedipal complex and a realistic portrait of provincial life.

### The Modernist Novel

In the late 1910s and early 1920s the novel evolved with Modernism. Joyce and Woolf reflected the loss of faith in traditional values, the trauma of World War I, and the disillusion with progress. Classical myths were freely incorporated into modern narratives: Joyce's Ulysses is built on Homer's Odyssey.

The greatest change was the breakdown of the division of time. Past, present and future were no longer separate: time became a continuous flux, and only individual consciousness could identify the significant moments. The technical solution was the stream of consciousness, also called interior monologue.

### Joyce and Woolf

James Joyce wrote Dubliners (1914), fifteen short stories whose central theme is paralysis — the inability to overcome mental frustration. With Ulysses (1922) he became one of the greatest modernist writers: the novel describes one ordinary day in the life of Leopold Bloom, using the epic model of the Odyssey to stress the lack of heroism in the modern world.

Virginia Woolf was the centre of the Bloomsbury Group. In her essay Modern Fiction (1919) she argued that human perception does not depend on measurable time ("time on the clock") but on inner time ("time in the mind"). In Mrs Dalloway (1925) a single day contains a whole life. In To the Lighthouse (1927) the female consciousness of Mrs Ramsay is contrasted with the male consciousness of her husband.

### The Colonial and Dystopian Novel

E.M. Forster, also part of the Bloomsbury Group, shared the modernist crisis of values, but his style is clear and not experimental. A Passage to India (1924) explores the difficult relations between the British and the Indians.

In the 1930s and 1940s the dystopian novel appeared. The most famous is George Orwell's Nineteen Eighty-Four (1949), where the dictator Big Brother controls people's thoughts through telescreens. Big Brother has become the symbol of modern man's enslavement to mass media.

## What Changes in Style

Modernist writing breaks with the past in clear ways:

- Plot: no longer linear, with a beginning, middle and end. Events are fragmented.
- Time: clock time is replaced by inner time. A single day can contain a whole life.
- Point of view: the omniscient narrator disappears. The reader is placed inside the mind of the characters.
- Language: complex, sometimes without punctuation, full of quotations and allusions in many languages.
- Myth: ancient myths are reused to read the chaos of modern life (the mythical method of Eliot and Joyce).
- Verse: free verse replaces traditional metres.
- Themes: the unconscious, the crisis of values, sterility, alienation, the loss of heroism.

!!! abstract "Modernism in one sentence"
    Modernism is the art of a world that has lost its certainties, and that tries to rebuild meaning by exploring the human mind and reusing the myths of the past.
