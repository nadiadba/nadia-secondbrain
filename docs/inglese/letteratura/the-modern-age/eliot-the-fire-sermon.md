# Eliot — The Fire Sermon

## Introduction

"The Fire Sermon" is the third section of *The Waste Land*. Eliot took the title from a famous sermon of Buddha, in which Buddha teaches that man must free himself from earthly things — desire, passion, possession — because only ascetism leads to true happiness. The title also recalls Baudelaire and his *Flowers of Evil*, an oxymoron that was very important for Eliot. In this section Eliot does not describe the modern world in an objective way: he uses symbols and short fragments to show us different love stories of his time. His aim is to make us think about love, and to show how modern love has been debased to near lust — love, the purest of feelings, is reduced to sex, which produces no fruit. For this reason the whole section is full of images of sterility. Eliot presents three kinds of love: Queen Elizabeth with the Count of Leicester, the Smyrna merchant, and the typist with a young clerk.

## The Smyrna merchant

The first scene shows Mr. Eugenides, the Smyrna merchant: an untidy man, unshaven, with a pocket full of currants. He asks the narrator, in a broken French, to have lunch at the Cannon Street Hotel and to spend a weekend at the Metropole, a hotel known as a place for homosexuals. The currants symbolise sterility, so Eliot is speaking of a homosexual love that cannot bear fruit.

## Tiresias

The next scene is introduced by Tiresias, the blind old prophet of Greek mythology. According to the myth, he once saw two snakes mating and separated them with a stick: for this he became a woman. Years later he saw them again, killed the male snake and returned a man. Hera, the wife of Zeus, blinded him because Tiresias had said that women feel more pleasure than men, and Zeus, to make up for this, gave him the gift of seeing beyond appearances. This is why in the poem Tiresias is a blind old man with wrinkled female breasts — now he is a man, but he was once a woman.

## The typist and the clerk

Tiresias watches a typist coming home from work at sunset. The food in tins, the clothes piled on the only divan where she sleeps at night, the stockings, camisoles and stays thrown around, all suggest dryness, old age and a careless life. Then her lover arrives: a small house agent's clerk with a bold stare — a vulgar upstart, a nouveau riche without any class. The meal is over, she is bored and tired, and he tries to convince her with caresses, then assaults her. She does not refuse, but she does not desire him either: her indifference becomes, for him, a welcome. Tiresias, who has already suffered everything and has sat by the walls of Thebes among the dead, foresuffers the whole scene on the same divan or bed. The man leaves with a final patronising kiss and gropes his way down the unlit, dark stairs. She turns and looks for a moment in the mirror, hardly aware that her lover has gone. One half-formed thought passes through her mind: "Well now that's done, and I'm glad it's over." Then she paces the room alone, smoothes her hair with an automatic hand, and puts a record on the gramophone.

## The meaning of the section

The title itself does not seem connected to the content of these scenes, but this is typical of Eliot's mythical method: he uses ancient myth not to contrast it with the present, but to place past and present side by side, because only through the past can we understand our own time (the same idea that Joyce uses with Molly and Penelope). The two couples Eliot describes are an objective correlative — concrete images and situations that evoke in the reader the same feeling of sterility and emptiness that the poet wants to communicate, without expressing it directly. This is also why Eliot is one of the greatest authors of Modernism.

## Collegamenti

- **Italiano**: Montale e la "poetica dell'oggetto" (*Ossi di seppia*) — il correlativo oggettivo arriva in Italia proprio da Eliot; la sterilità e l'aridità del paesaggio ligure richiamano la "waste land"
- **Filosofia**: Schopenhauer e la vita come dolore, il buddhismo e l'ascesi come liberazione dal desiderio
- **Storia**: la crisi dei valori dopo la Prima guerra mondiale, la società di massa e la perdita di senso
- **Latino/Greco**: il mito di Tiresia (Ovidio, *Metamorfosi*, libro III), la *Divina Commedia* di Dante (Tiresia è tra gli indovini dell'*Inferno*, canto XX)
- **Arte**: la tecnica del collage nelle avanguardie (Cubismo, Dadaismo) — immagini accostate senza nesso logico
