# Joyce — The Dead and Ulysses

## The Dead

"The Dead" is the last and longest story in *Dubliners*. It is the climax of the whole collection. Unlike the other stories, which focus on individual paralysis, "The Dead" brings together many characters and offers a more compassionate view of Dublin.

### The Story

The story begins with a party that the elderly Morkan sisters organise each year at the Epiphany. They invite friends and relatives, both Catholics and Protestants, and the guests discuss Irish politics and the need for political commitment.

The central character is Gabriel Conroy, nephew to the Morkan sisters. He is an intelligent and successful journalist, but he has an unfulfilled personality: he would like to take sides on Ireland's problems, to travel to Europe (like Joyce himself), and to write everything he feels he could write. Instead, he prefers not to expose himself and continues his usual life.

Late at night, when the party is over, Gabriel and his wife Gretta go back to their hotel. Gabriel is excited and desires his wife. But Gretta is lost in thought, and the explanation will come as a bitter surprise.

### "I Think He Died for Me"

At the hotel, Gabriel kisses Gretta. His heart is full of happiness. But when he asks "Gretta dear, what are you thinking about?", she does not answer. Then she says in an outburst of tears: "O, I am thinking about that song, *The Lass of Aughrim*" — a song she heard at the party. She breaks free from him, runs to the bed and hides her face.

Gabriel learns that Gretta is thinking about a young boy she used to know long ago, named Michael Furey. He used to sing that song. He was very delicate, with big dark eyes. They used to go out walking together when she was in Galway.

Gabriel feels humiliated. While he had been full of tenderness, joy and desire, she had been comparing him in her mind with another man. He sees himself as a ridiculous figure — "a pennyboy for his aunts, a nervous well-meaning sentimentalist."

Gretta reveals that Michael Furey was ill with consumption (tuberculosis). When the time came for her to leave Galway, he came to see her in the rain, standing at the end of the garden, shivering. She told him to go home, but he said he did not want to live. A week later, when she was at the convent, he died. He was only seventeen.

"I think he died for me," she answered.

### "The Living and the Dead"

This is the concluding part of the story. Gretta is asleep. Gabriel watches her and thinks about how poor a part he, her husband, has played in her life. A man had died for her sake. He watches her as though they had never lived together as man and wife.

A strange friendly pity enters his soul. He knows that her face is no longer beautiful, but he also knows it is no longer the face for which Michael Furey had braved death.

Gabriel lies down beside his wife. One by one they were all becoming shades. He thinks:

> "Better pass boldly into that other world, in the full glory of some passion, than fade and wither dismally with age."

Generous tears fill Gabriel's eyes. He has never felt like that himself towards any woman, but he knows that such a feeling must be love. In the partial darkness he imagines a young man standing under a dripping tree.

His soul has approached that region where the vast hosts of the dead dwell. His own identity is fading out into a grey impalpable world. He hears the snow falling faintly through the universe and faintly falling, like the descent of their last end, upon all the living and the dead.

!!! abstract "The Epiphany in The Dead"
    Gabriel's epiphany is the most complex in *Dubliners*. The snow falling outside the window makes him realise that he has never experienced true love — not the kind of selfless passion that Michael Furey felt. The snow is the central symbol: it falls on everyone, on the living and the dead, uniting them in a common destiny. Gabriel's identity dissolves as he understands the limitations of his own emotional life.

!!! note "The Snow as Symbol"
    The snow represents several things at once: death, purification, stillness, and above all a common destiny shared by all people. It falls "upon all the living and the dead," erasing the boundary between them. It is one of the most famous endings in English literature.

## Ulysses (1922)

### What Kind of Novel Is It?

*Ulysses* is a modernist novel set in Dublin on a single day — 16 June 1904. Its title refers to the mythical hero of Homer's *Odyssey*, and its eighteen chapters are closely modelled on episodes from the ancient epic. Joyce began thinking about *Ulysses* when he was in Rome. The novel was completed over seven years in three different cities (Trieste, Zurich, Paris) and published in Paris in 1922.

### The Story

*Ulysses* tells the story of a day in the life of three characters.

Leopold Bloom is a Dubliner of Jewish origin who works as an advertising agent. He is the Ulysses of the title and wanders about the streets of Dublin just as Homer's Ulysses wandered about the Mediterranean. What happens to Bloom is, however, quite banal: he gets up, goes to a Turkish bath, attends a funeral, goes to his newspaper office, has lunch, wanders about, goes to the library, buys things, meets people, goes to a pub, sits at a beach, visits a hospital, and ends up at a brothel. At midnight he meets Stephen Dedalus and takes him home.

Stephen Dedalus is the central character of the first part. He is a sensitive young man with literary ambitions who feels frustrated by Irish provincial life. He is looking for a father figure, which he eventually finds in Bloom. Homeless and fatherless, he corresponds to Telemachus, the son of Ulysses.

Molly Bloom, Leopold's wife, is the central character of the last part. She corresponds to Ulysses' wife Penelope, but unlike Penelope she is unfaithful to her husband. The novel ends with her famous stream-of-consciousness monologue as she lies awake in bed.

### The Mythical Structure

Each chapter of *Ulysses* corresponds to an episode in the *Odyssey*: the modern Nestor is headmaster of the school where Stephen teaches; Bloom's trip to a funeral is a visit to the underworld; the Sirens are barmaids; and Molly's thoughts at night reveal an unlikely (that is, unfaithful) Penelope.

Joyce uses the epic model to show the lack of heroism, of ideals, of love and of trust in the modern world. The epic structure becomes a mirror in which to reflect the modern wasteland. Together with Eliot's *The Waste Land*, *Ulysses* is one of the greatest examples of the reworking of myth in modernist literature.

!!! info "The Linati Schema"
    On 21 September 1920, Joyce sent a schema of the novel to his Italian friend Carlo Linati. In it, all the chapters were given a title corresponding to an episode in the *Odyssey* (Calipso, Eolo, Scilla e Cariddi, Sirene, Ciclope, Itaca, Penelope...). Joyce also gave indications for the interpretation of each chapter: the time of day, the characters, the technique, the organ of the body, the colour, the symbol, and the art.

### Why Is Ulysses a Difficult Novel?

*Ulysses* is famous for being written using the stream-of-consciousness technique. This is a difficult prose style which removes syntactic and grammatical connectives and puts together disparate images, in an attempt to show the chaotic flow of thoughts in the human mind.

It was a technical innovation meant to reproduce the uninterrupted flow of thoughts, as they first come to a person's mind before they are organised logically. Joyce adopted this style to convey the life of an individual on a single day by giving the minutest details of that day, and especially the process of the character's thinking.

The term "stream of consciousness" was originally coined by the American philosopher William James (1842–1910). Joyce's technique includes several devices:

- Short compressed sentences, often without a main verb, to show the stream of thoughts
- Events described in a fragmentary way, interrupted by side comments or unrelated thoughts
- Abrupt images from a character's past with little connection to the present
- Questions in the mind, not meant for anyone, to convey the chaotic flow of thoughts
- A musical quality — assonance and alliteration that reproduce the mind's fascination with sounds

### "Yes I Said Yes I Will Yes" — Molly's Monologue

The final episode of *Ulysses* is entitled "Penelope" and is Molly Bloom's famous interior monologue. The stream-of-consciousness technique is taken to its extreme: no external events are described, there is no third-person narrator, and there is no punctuation at all — no paragraphs, no commas, no full stops. Nothing suggests an order or a hierarchy of thoughts.

The technique is used to show Molly's sleepy sensuality from the inside. She is half thinking, half dreaming of the day Bloom proposed to her, together with memories of her youth in Gibraltar, other kisses and other men. Her last words are the climax of both her stream of consciousness and her erotic fantasies.

The passage begins with "the sun shines for you he said the day we were lying among the rhododendrons on Howth head" — a memory of the day Bloom proposed to her. Molly recalls how "I gave him the bit of seedcake out of my mouth" and ends with "yes I said yes I will Yes." The final "Yes" is one of the most famous words in all of literature: an affirmation of life, of love, of sensuality, of acceptance.

!!! note "Why No Punctuation?"
    Joyce deliberately removes all punctuation to reproduce the continuous, chaotic, unfiltered flow of thoughts passing through a half-sleeping mind. There is no way to interrupt this flux of free associations, memories and fantasies. The reader is placed directly inside Molly's consciousness, with no external narrator to provide guidance.

### Censorship and Publication

*Ulysses* had serious problems with censorship. Serialisation began in the *American Little Review* in 1918 but was stopped when the "Nausicaa" chapter was considered obscene. The Hogarth Press (owned by Leonard and Virginia Woolf) also turned it down. It was only through the efforts of Sylvia Beach, owner of the Shakespeare & Co. Bookshop in Paris, and of Pound and Eliot, that the novel was eventually published.

Copies were confiscated and destroyed at the US customs house. In the US the novel could not be distributed until 1933. In England it was banned until 1936, and in Ireland it was not freely available until the 1960s.
