# Joyce — The Dead and Ulysses

## The Dead

"The Dead" is the last and longest story in *Dubliners* and the climax of the whole collection. Unlike the other stories, which focus on individual paralysis, "The Dead" brings together many characters and offers a more compassionate view of Dublin.

The story begins with a party that the elderly Morkan sisters organise each year at the Epiphany. They invite friends and relatives, both Catholics and Protestants, and the guests discuss Irish politics. The central character is Gabriel Conroy, nephew to the Morkan sisters. He is an intelligent journalist, but he has an unfulfilled personality: he would like to take sides on Ireland's problems, travel to Europe, and write freely, but he prefers not to expose himself and continues his usual life. Late at night, when the party is over, Gabriel and his wife Gretta go back to their hotel. Gabriel desires his wife, but Gretta is lost in thought.

### "I Think He Died for Me"

At the hotel, Gabriel kisses Gretta and his heart is full of happiness. He puts his hands on her hair and begins smoothing it back, scarcely touching it with his fingers. He is sure she feels the same desire he feels. But when he asks "Gretta dear, what are you thinking about?", she does not answer. He asks again, softly. Then she says in an outburst of tears: "O, I am thinking about that song, *The Lass of Aughrim*" — a song she heard at the party. She breaks free from him and runs to the bed, throwing her arms across the bed-rail to hide her face. Gabriel stands still for a moment in astonishment.

He follows her and asks why the song makes her cry. She raises her head and dries her eyes with the back of her hand, like a child. She tells him she is thinking about a person she used to know long ago, a young boy named Michael Furey. He used to sing that song. He was very delicate, with big dark eyes, and they used to go out walking together when she was in Galway. Gabriel asks if she was in love with him, and she says "I was great with him at that time."

Gabriel feels humiliated by the thought of this figure from the dead — a boy in the gasworks. While he had been full of memories of tenderness, joy and desire, she had been comparing him in her mind with another man. A dull anger begins to gather at the back of his mind. He sees himself as a ridiculous figure — "a pennyboy for his aunts, a nervous well-meaning sentimentalist, orating to vulgarians and idealising his own clownish lusts." He catches a glimpse of himself in the mirror and turns his back to the light, ashamed.

Gretta reveals that Michael Furey was ill with consumption (tuberculosis). He was only seventeen. When the time came for her to leave Galway and go to the convent in Dublin, he was very ill. She wrote him a letter saying she would be back in the summer. But the night before she left, she heard gravel thrown up against her window. She ran downstairs and there was the poor fellow, at the end of the garden, shivering in the rain. She told him to go home at once or he would get his death. But he said he did not want to live. She can see his eyes as well as she can see Gabriel's. A week later, at the convent, she heard that he was dead. "I think he died for me," she answered.

### "The Living and the Dead"

This is the concluding part. Gretta has cried herself to sleep. Gabriel, leaning on his elbow, looks at her for a few moments without resentment. So she had had that romance in her life: a man had died for her sake. It hardly pains him now to think how poor a part he, her husband, has played in her life. He watches her while she sleeps as though he and she had never lived together as man and wife. His curious eyes rest long upon her face and on her hair, and as he thinks of what she must have been then, in that time of her first girlish beauty, a strange friendly pity enters his soul. He does not like to say even to himself that her face is no longer beautiful, but he knows that it is no longer the face for which Michael Furey had braved death.

The air of the room chills his shoulders. He stretches himself cautiously along under the sheets and lies down beside his wife. One by one they were all becoming shades. He thinks: "Better pass boldly into that other world, in the full glory of some passion, than fade and wither dismally with age." He thinks of how the boy who lay beside Gretta in her heart for so many years had told her that he did not wish to live. Generous tears fill Gabriel's eyes. He has never felt like that himself towards any woman, but he knows that such a feeling must be love. The tears gather more thickly in his eyes and in the partial darkness he imagines a young man standing under a dripping tree. Other forms are near.

His soul has approached that region where the vast hosts of the dead dwell. He is conscious of, but cannot understand, their wayward and flickering existence. His own identity is fading out into a grey impalpable world. A few light taps upon the window pane make him turn to the window. It has begun to snow again. He watches sleepily the flakes, silver and dark, falling obliquely against the lamplight. The time has come for him to set out on his journey westward. Snow is falling on every part of the dark central plain, on the treeless hills, falling softly upon the Bog of Allen and, farther westward, softly falling into the dark mutinous Shannon waves. It is falling, too, upon every part of the lonely churchyard on the hill where Michael Furey lies buried. It lies thickly on the crooked crosses and headstones, on the spears of the little gate, on the barren thorns. His soul swoones slowly as he hears the snow falling faintly through the universe and faintly falling, like the descent of their last end, upon all the living and the dead.

!!! abstract "The Epiphany in The Dead"
    Gabriel's epiphany is the most complex in *Dubliners*. The snow falling outside makes him realise he has never experienced true love — not the selfless passion that Michael Furey felt. The snow is the central symbol: it falls on the living and the dead, uniting them in a common destiny.

!!! note "The Snow as Symbol"
    The snow represents death, purification, stillness, and above all a common destiny shared by all people. It erases the boundary between the living and the dead. It is one of the most famous endings in English literature.

## Ulysses (1922)

*Ulysses* is a modernist novel set in Dublin on a single day — 16 June 1904. Its title refers to Homer's *Odyssey*, and its eighteen chapters are modelled on episodes from the ancient epic. Joyce completed the novel over seven years in Trieste, Zurich and Paris, and published it in Paris in 1922.

### The Story

The novel tells the story of a day in the life of three characters. Leopold Bloom is a Dubliner of Jewish origin who works as an advertising agent. He is the Ulysses of the title and wanders about Dublin just as Homer's Ulysses wandered about the Mediterranean. His day is quite banal: he gets up, goes to a Turkish bath, attends a funeral, goes to his newspaper office, has lunch, wanders about, goes to the library, meets people, goes to a pub, sits at a beach, visits a hospital, and ends up at a brothel where at midnight he meets Stephen Dedalus and takes him home.

Stephen Dedalus is the central character of the first part — a sensitive young man with literary ambitions who feels frustrated by Irish provincial life. He is looking for a father figure, which he finds in Bloom. Homeless and fatherless, he corresponds to Telemachus, the son of Ulysses. Molly Bloom, Leopold's wife, is the central character of the last part. She corresponds to Penelope, but unlike Penelope she is unfaithful. The novel ends with her famous stream-of-consciousness monologue.

### The Mythical Structure

Each chapter corresponds to an episode in the *Odyssey*: the modern Nestor is headmaster of a school; Bloom's trip to a funeral is a visit to the underworld; the Sirens are barmaids; Molly's thoughts at night reveal an unfaithful Penelope. Joyce uses the epic model to show the lack of heroism, ideals, love and trust in the modern world. Together with Eliot's *The Waste Land*, *Ulysses* is one of the greatest examples of the reworking of myth in modernist literature.

!!! info "The Linati Schema"
    In 1920, Joyce sent a schema of the novel to his Italian friend Carlo Linati. Each chapter was given a title from the *Odyssey* (Calipso, Eolo, Sirene, Ciclope, Itaca, Penelope...) and Joyce gave indications for its interpretation: the time of day, the characters, the technique, the organ of the body, the colour, the symbol, and the art.

### The Stream of Consciousness

*Ulysses* is famous for being written using the stream-of-consciousness technique — a prose style which removes syntactic connectives and puts together disparate images to show the chaotic flow of thoughts in the human mind. The term was coined by the American philosopher William James (1842–1910). Joyce's technique includes: short sentences without a main verb; events interrupted by unrelated thoughts; abrupt images from the past; questions not meant for anyone; and a musical quality based on assonance and alliteration.

### "Yes I Said Yes I Will Yes" — Molly's Monologue

The final episode, entitled "Penelope," is Molly Bloom's famous interior monologue. The stream-of-consciousness technique is taken to its extreme: there is no third-person narrator and no punctuation at all — no paragraphs, no commas, no full stops. Nothing suggests an order or a hierarchy of thoughts.

Molly is lying in bed, half thinking, half dreaming. Her thoughts move freely between the present and the past, mixing memories, fantasies and desires with no logical order. She thinks about the day Bloom proposed to her on Howth Head, a hill near Dublin. They were lying among the rhododendrons, and she gave him a bit of seedcake out of her mouth. She remembers her youth in Gibraltar — the Spanish girls laughing in their shawls, the old castle, the handsome Moors in white, the little shops with their old windows, the glorious sunsets and the sea "sometimes like fire." She thinks about other kisses and other men, mixing everything together in one continuous flow.

The passage ends with the famous words "yes I said yes I will Yes" — Molly's memory of the moment she accepted Bloom's proposal. The final "Yes" is one of the most famous words in all of literature: an affirmation of life, of love, of sensuality, of acceptance. It is important to notice that Molly says "I got him to propose to me," suggesting that she was the one who made it happen, not Bloom.

!!! note "Why No Punctuation?"
    Joyce removes all punctuation to reproduce the continuous, chaotic flow of thoughts in a half-sleeping mind. The reader is placed directly inside Molly's consciousness, with no narrator to provide guidance.

### Censorship and Publication

*Ulysses* had serious problems with censorship. Serialisation began in the *American Little Review* in 1918 but was stopped when a chapter was considered obscene. The Hogarth Press (owned by Leonard and Virginia Woolf) also turned it down. The novel was eventually published thanks to Sylvia Beach, owner of the Shakespeare & Co. Bookshop in Paris. Copies were confiscated at the US customs house. In the US it could not be distributed until 1933, in England until 1936, and in Ireland until the 1960s.
