# Joyce — The Dead and Ulysses

## The Dead

"The Dead" is the last and longest story in *Dubliners* and the climax of the whole collection. Unlike the other stories, which focus on individual paralysis, "The Dead" brings together many characters and offers a more compassionate view of Dublin.

The story begins with a party that the elderly Morkan sisters organise each year at the Epiphany. They invite friends and relatives, both Catholics and Protestants, and the guests discuss Irish politics. The central character is Gabriel Conroy, nephew to the Morkan sisters. He is an intelligent journalist, but he has an unfulfilled personality: he would like to take sides on Ireland's problems, travel to Europe, and write freely, but he prefers not to expose himself and continues his usual life. Late at night, when the party is over, Gabriel and his wife Gretta go back to their hotel. Gabriel desires his wife, but Gretta is lost in thought.

### "I Think He Died for Me"

At the hotel, Gabriel kisses Gretta and feels happy. But when he asks what she is thinking about, she bursts into tears: "O, I am thinking about that song, *The Lass of Aughrim*" — a song she heard at the party. She runs to the bed and hides her face.

Gabriel learns that Gretta is thinking about a young boy she used to know in Galway, named Michael Furey. He used to sing that song. He was very delicate, with big dark eyes. Gabriel feels humiliated: while he had been full of tenderness and desire, she had been comparing him with another man. He sees himself as a ridiculous figure — "a pennyboy for his aunts, a nervous well-meaning sentimentalist."

Gretta reveals that Michael Furey was ill with consumption (tuberculosis). When she had to leave Galway, he came to see her in the rain, standing at the end of the garden, shivering. She told him to go home, but he said he did not want to live. A week later he died. He was only seventeen. "I think he died for me," she answered.

### "The Living and the Dead"

This is the concluding part. Gretta is asleep. Gabriel watches her and thinks about how poor a part he has played in her life. A man had died for her sake. He watches her as though they had never lived together as man and wife. A strange friendly pity enters his soul.

Gabriel lies down beside his wife. One by one they were all becoming shades. He thinks: "Better pass boldly into that other world, in the full glory of some passion, than fade and wither dismally with age." Generous tears fill his eyes. He has never felt like that towards any woman, but he knows that such a feeling must be love. In the darkness he imagines a young man standing under a dripping tree.

His soul has approached that region where the vast hosts of the dead dwell. His own identity is fading out into a grey impalpable world. He hears the snow falling faintly through the universe and faintly falling, like the descent of their last end, upon all the living and the dead.

!!! abstract "The Epiphany in The Dead"
    Gabriel's epiphany is the most complex in *Dubliners*. The snow falling outside makes him realise he has never experienced true love — not the selfless passion that Michael Furey felt. The snow is the central symbol: it falls on the living and the dead, uniting them in a common destiny.

!!! note "The Snow as Symbol"
    The snow represents death, purification, stillness, and above all a common destiny shared by all people. It erases the boundary between the living and the dead. It is one of the most famous endings in English literature.

## Ulysses (1922)

*Ulysses* is a modernist novel set in Dublin on a single day — 16 June 1904. Its title refers to Homer's *Odyssey*, and its eighteen chapters are modelled on episodes from the ancient epic. Joyce completed the novel over seven years in Trieste, Zurich and Paris, and published it in Paris in 1922.

### The Story

The novel tells the story of a day in the life of three characters. Leopold Bloom is a Dubliner of Jewish origin who works as an advertising agent. He is the Ulysses of the title and wanders about Dublin just as Homer's Ulysses wandered about the Mediterranean. His day is quite banal: he gets up, goes to a Turkish bath, attends a funeral, goes to his newspaper office, has lunch, wanders about, goes to the library, meets people, goes to a pub, sits at a beach, visits a hospital, and ends up at a brothel where at midnight he meets Stephen Dedalus and takes him home.

Stephen Dedalus is the central character of the first part — a sensitive young man with literary ambitions who feels frustrated by Irish provincial life. He is looking for a father figure, which he finds in Bloom. Homeless and fatherless, he corresponds to Telemachus, the son of Ulysses. Molly Bloom, Leopold's wife, is the central character of the last part. She corresponds to Penelope, but unlike Penelope she is unfaithful. The novel ends with her famous stream-of-consciousness monologue.

### The Mythical Structure

Each chapter corresponds to an episode in the *Odyssey*: the modern Nestor is headmaster of a school; Bloom's trip to a funeral is a visit to the underworld; the Sirens are barmaids; Molly's thoughts at night reveal an unfaithful Penelope. Joyce uses the epic model to show the lack of heroism, ideals, love and trust in the modern world. Together with Eliot's *The Waste Land*, *Ulysses* is one of the greatest examples of the reworking of myth in modernist literature.

!!! info "The Linati Schema"
    In 1920, Joyce sent a schema of the novel to his Italian friend Carlo Linati. Each chapter was given a title from the *Odyssey* (Calipso, Eolo, Sirene, Ciclope, Itaca, Penelope...) and Joyce gave indications for its interpretation: the time of day, the characters, the technique, the organ of the body, the colour, the symbol, and the art.

### The Stream of Consciousness

*Ulysses* is famous for being written using the stream-of-consciousness technique — a prose style which removes syntactic connectives and puts together disparate images to show the chaotic flow of thoughts in the human mind. The term was coined by the American philosopher William James (1842–1910). Joyce's technique includes: short sentences without a main verb; events interrupted by unrelated thoughts; abrupt images from the past; questions not meant for anyone; and a musical quality based on assonance and alliteration.

### "Yes I Said Yes I Will Yes" — Molly's Monologue

The final episode, entitled "Penelope," is Molly Bloom's famous interior monologue. The stream-of-consciousness technique is taken to its extreme: there is no third-person narrator and no punctuation at all — no paragraphs, no commas, no full stops. Molly is half thinking, half dreaming of the day Bloom proposed to her, together with memories of her youth in Gibraltar, other kisses and other men. The passage ends with "yes I said yes I will Yes" — one of the most famous words in literature, an affirmation of life, love and acceptance.

!!! note "Why No Punctuation?"
    Joyce removes all punctuation to reproduce the continuous, chaotic flow of thoughts in a half-sleeping mind. The reader is placed directly inside Molly's consciousness, with no narrator to provide guidance.

### Censorship and Publication

*Ulysses* had serious problems with censorship. Serialisation began in the *American Little Review* in 1918 but was stopped when a chapter was considered obscene. The Hogarth Press (owned by Leonard and Virginia Woolf) also turned it down. The novel was eventually published thanks to Sylvia Beach, owner of the Shakespeare & Co. Bookshop in Paris. Copies were confiscated at the US customs house. In the US it could not be distributed until 1933, in England until 1936, and in Ireland until the 1960s.
