# T.S. Eliot (1888–1965)

## Life and Works

T.S. Eliot was the leading poet of Modernism in the 1910s and 1920s. In 1922 — the same year in which Joyce's *Ulysses* and Woolf's *Mrs Dalloway* project began — he published *The Waste Land*, a long poem that includes quotations in six different languages and adaptations from Dante, Baudelaire and many other writers, following a collage technique typical of Modernism.

Eliot was born in the US but lived most of his life in England. He was a great experimenter but also a classicist. He started with a deeply pessimistic view of life, which later turned into serene Christian acceptance. He received the Nobel Prize for literature in 1948.

### American Roots

Thomas Stearns Eliot was born in 1888 into an affluent family of St Louis, Missouri. The Eliots descended from New England Puritans who had migrated to America in the 17th century. His grandfather was a Unitarian Minister who had founded a college — later to become Washington University. A strong sense of civic and religious duty was part of the family tradition.

### Education

In 1906 Eliot went to Harvard, where he studied Renaissance literature, philosophy, Hindu and Buddhist philosophy, and Sanskrit — all of which were to enter *The Waste Land*. He graduated with a dissertation on the philosopher F.H. Bradley, whose ideas on the psychological nature of individual experience influenced Eliot's poetry.

After Harvard, Eliot went to the Sorbonne in Paris (1910), where he studied the philosophy of Henri Bergson and began to read the French Symbolist poets. He then travelled to Germany. At the beginning of World War I, in 1914, he moved to Merton College, Oxford.

### Beginnings as a Poet

1915 was a crucial year for Eliot. He moved to London, taught in a school for a while, and then went to work at Lloyd's Bank (a position he held until 1925 but never liked). In the same year he married Vivienne Haigh-Wood, a talented woman afflicted by bad health and bad nerves.

He had begun to write but was dissatisfied with the poetry of the time, which seemed to him imprecise and out of touch with the modern world. The meeting with his fellow American poet Ezra Pound and the poetry of Imagism, with its insistence on clear and precise images, was decisive. Through Pound's influence, the magazine *Poetry* published Eliot's first major poem, *The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock* (1915), followed by *Poems* (1919). These early poems deal with the emptiness of modern upper-class life and the difficulty of communication, and owe much to the French Symbolists.

### The Waste Land and the Nihilistic Phase

The years 1915–25 were very hard for Eliot. Money problems, working full-time in a bank, poor relations with his wife, his own nervous instability, and a general dissatisfaction with modern life — all of these combined to bring him to a nervous breakdown in November 1921. He went for rest first to Margate, southern England, and then to a Swiss sanatorium in Lausanne.

During this time he finished the first draft of *The Waste Land*. On his way back to London he stopped in Paris to submit the manuscript to Ezra Pound, who made extensive cuts and helped him give the poem its final shape. *The Waste Land* came out in 1922, making Eliot the leading modernist poet writing in English.

He left the bank and in 1925 became a director of the publishing house Faber and Gwyer (now Faber and Faber), publishing the works of important modern poets including Ezra Pound and W.H. Auden.

The central metaphor of this nihilistic phase — which also includes *Gerontion* (1919) and *The Hollow Men* (1925) — is one of sterility, aridity, impotence.

### Religion and the Christian Phase

The late 1920s and the 1930s were important years for Eliot, who found a way out of nihilism in religion. He was officially received into the Anglican Church in 1927, the year in which he also became a British subject. This is reflected in the poems of the late 1920s: *The Journey of the Magi* (1927) and *Ash Wednesday* (1930). In them we find a quiet and hopeful searching for a higher truth.

This reached its culmination with his play *Murder in the Cathedral* (1935), based on the martyrdom of Thomas Becket, and with *Four Quartets* — a set of four related poems: *Burnt Norton* (1935), *East Coker* (1940), *The Dry Salvages* (1943), *Little Gidding* (1942). All four were published together in 1943. Written during World War II, they are perhaps his most personal works, since each of them is named after a place of special significance for Eliot.

### Late Years

In 1948 he received the Nobel Prize for literature and the British Order of Merit. His wife Vivienne had died the previous year; he had separated from her in 1933, and in 1937 she had been committed to a mental institution.

After World War II, Eliot was mostly occupied with drama. He developed a new kind of drama combining religious themes with contemporary subjects and the language of drawing-room conversation. His later plays — *The Cocktail Party* (1949), *The Confidential Clerk* (1954), and *The Elder Statesman* (1959) — treat religious and moral themes through sophisticated social comedies.

Eliot married again in 1957. He died in London in 1965.

## Eliot's Modernism

Eliot and Ezra Pound were the first great modernist poets of the English language. To the "waste land" of the modern world, Eliot opposed Western culture — the Middle Ages, the Renaissance, Indian philosophy, French Symbolism, modern music including jazz, and finally the Christian religion.

Eliot's poetry is difficult, and he himself believed that in our time art should be difficult. His poetry has two kinds of difficulty:

- The poems of the nihilistic phase (up to *The Hollow Men*) are difficult because he removes connective and transitional passages, putting disparate images or half-disguised quotations side by side
- The poems of the Christian phase are more traditional in structure, but their philosophical background is complex and Eliot often plays on the meaning and origin of words

### Poetical Sources

Eliot learned from the Imagists (Ezra Pound) the necessity for clear and precise language and images, and an economy of words.

From the French Symbolists (especially Baudelaire) he took free verse and the idea that the sordidness and ugliness of modern city life could be subjects for poetry.

He was deeply influenced by John Donne and the Metaphysical poets, whom he helped to re-evaluate. He admired their use of wit, high rhetoric, and daring images taken from different traditions.

Perhaps the single greatest influence on Eliot was Dante. Eliot found in the *Divina Commedia* the expression not of an individual sensibility but of a whole civilisation, and a complete range of emotion from hell's despair to heaven's beatitude.

!!! abstract "Objective Correlative"
    One of Eliot's most important critical concepts: "a set of objects, a situation, a chain of events which shall be the formula of that particular emotion; such that when the external facts are given, the emotion is immediately evoked." In other words, the poet should not express emotions directly but find concrete images that will produce the same emotion in the reader.

!!! abstract "Dissociation of Sensibility"
    Another key concept: the separation of intellect and emotion that, according to Eliot, modern poetry lacked. This unity could be found instead in the Metaphysical poets, who were able to "feel their thought as immediately as the odour of a rose."

### Literary and Social Criticism

Eliot was also a very important literary critic. His most influential essay, *Tradition and the Individual Talent* (1919), outlines the Modernists' attitude to the art of the past: a writer should have "a perception, not only of the pastness of the past, but of its presence" — the whole of European literature from Homer onwards has a simultaneous existence.

In *For Lancelot Andrewes* (1928), Eliot described himself as "classicist in literature, royalist in politics, and Anglo-Catholic in religion."

His social criticism is also important. Essays like *The Idea of a Christian Society* (1939) and *Notes Towards a Definition of Culture* (1943) raise fundamental questions about modern society and the way we live.

## The Waste Land (1922)

### The Central Modernist Work

*The Waste Land* is perhaps the central work in modernist literature. Its influence on contemporary and future poets has been immense. The poem is divided into five sections:

1. "The Burial of the Dead" (lines 1–76)
2. "A Game of Chess" (lines 77–172)
3. "The Fire Sermon" (lines 173–311)
4. "Death by Water" (lines 312–21)
5. "What the Thunder Said" (lines 322–433)

### Cultural and Spiritual Sterility

*The Waste Land* is considered by many the most important poem of the 20th century. It expresses the modern artist's disillusion with the modern world and, at the same time, the desperate need for a new tradition. It represents the culmination of the nihilistic phase in Eliot's career: the poet sees only ruin and desolation around him and is concerned with the decay of Western civilisation that followed World War I.

*The Waste Land* cannot be reconstructed into a coherent, logically ordered narrative: images are juxtaposed, not logically ordered. The fragmentation of the poem reflects the fragmentation of contemporary culture, in which each individual must try to find a personal ordering or interpretation.

!!! warning "The Central Metaphor"
    The central metaphor of the poem is the spiritual dryness and sterility of modern life — the death of Western culture through a lack of any belief, religious or other, that can give meaning to everyday existence.

### The Mythical Structure

To express this theme, *The Waste Land* brings together images of modern decadence with images, echoes and quotations from ancient myths and legends. Eliot referred his readers to two important sources: Sir James Frazer's study of pagan myths and fertility rites, *The Golden Bough* (1890), and Jessie L. Weston's *From Ritual to Romance* (1920), which relates ancient fertility myths to the medieval romances of the Holy Grail.

In these romances we see a wasteland whose ruler, the Fisher King, has brought sterility to the land because of his impotence or death. The many references to sterility and fertility provide a framework for all the fragments of the poem. The order of myth is projected on to the chaos of modern life.

### Eliot's Method

The structure of *The Waste Land* is modernist: its five unequal sections show no realistic or logical continuity. The time-shifts and the lack of a narrative sequence reveal Eliot's stream-of-consciousness technique, based on free association of thoughts.

Eliot uses quotations from or allusions to thirty-five writers, in six languages, as well as frequent allusions to various philosophical and religious traditions. The use of a medieval poet like Dante to express something fundamental to modern sensibility is typical: the crowds flowing over London Bridge at the end of "The Burial of the Dead" are modelled on the crowds of damned souls in Dante's *Inferno*.

The metrical pattern reflects the poem's variety: lines vary in length and rhythm in a form of free verse, although there are some regularities and rhymes.
