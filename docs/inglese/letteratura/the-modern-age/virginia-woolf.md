# Virginia Woolf (1882–1941)

## Life and Works

Virginia Woolf perfectly represents, as a woman and a writer, the transition between the Victorian and the Modern Age. She was born in London in 1882, the third child of **Sir Leslie Stephen**, a distinguished Victorian literary critic, and Julia Prinsep Jackson. Her sister **Vanessa** became an important modernist painter. Virginia grew up in a household full of people devoted to intellectual activities of all kinds — in her own words, "born of well-to-do parents, born into a very communicative, literate, letter writing, visiting, articulate, late nineteenth century world." However, as was the rule for girls of her social class, while her brothers went to university, Virginia was mostly educated at home. She was fully aware that a higher education for women was not what society expected: "A girl had no chance against its fangs. No other desires — say to paint, or to write — could be taken seriously."

Her childhood began happily at her parents' big house at St Ives, Cornwall, but it came to an abrupt end in **1895** when her mother died, followed by a long period of depression. When her half-sister Stella died in 1897, things got worse. These were the first signs of the nervous fragility that accompanied Virginia throughout her life, resulting in periodical fits of mental instability. Her father's death in **1904** produced another period of deep depression and her first attempt at suicide.

After her father's death, the Stephens moved from Kensington to **Bloomsbury**, where their house became a centre for literary, artistic and philosophical meetings. This group of writers and artists became known as the **Bloomsbury Group** — they were anti-Victorian, unconventional in their ideas about life and art, and sceptical about religion. Among them were the philosopher Clive Bell, the novelist E.M. Forster, the economist John Maynard Keynes, and **Leonard Woolf**, a novelist and journalist whom Virginia married in **1912**. In 1917 the couple founded the **Hogarth Press**, which became a major force in English publishing, printing not only Virginia's works but also T.S. Eliot's poems and the English translations of Freud.

Woolf's progress as a novelist reflects her development from realistic to Modernist fiction. Her first two novels, *The Voyage Out* (1915) and *Night and Day* (1919), were still fairly conventional. Her mature work began with *Jacob's Room* (1922), written using the **stream-of-consciousness** technique. Her subsequent novels — *Mrs Dalloway* (1925), *To the Lighthouse* (1927), *Orlando* (1928) and *The Waves* (1931) — all followed this model, based on the idea that a novel could not make sense of life simply through realistic description and a conventional plot. Like Joyce, Woolf preferred short meaningful time units: one day in *Mrs Dalloway*, a few hours in *Between the Acts*. These short units are expanded beyond their limits by what goes on within the characters' minds, which can cover years and range from past to present to future.

Throughout her life, Woolf was also deeply interested in the **Woman Question**. As early as 1910 she was working as a volunteer in the women's suffrage movement, and her most famous essay, *A Room of One's Own* (1929), became a milestone of modern feminism.

In her later years, Virginia became more subject to crises caused by anxiety. When World War II began, the Woolfs moved to Sussex, but the situation did not improve. Unable to face the terror and destruction around her, she drowned herself in the **River Ouse** on 28 March 1941. Psychiatrists today believe she was affected by **bipolar disorder**.

## A Room of One's Own (1929)

In October 1928, Virginia Woolf gave two lectures at Newnham College and Girton College, two women's colleges at the University of Cambridge. These lectures, delivered to audiences of mostly women on the subject of **women and literature**, were later published in essay form as *A Room of One's Own*.

The title comes from Woolf's most celebrated image of what women lacked in order to be as good as men at writing: simply **a room, time, and a decent income** — the freedom to write without having to bother about managing a household all of the time. Woolf's arguments are clearly presented: in the past, women had no existence except in the fiction written by men. Even if a woman had the same gifts as a man, social disadvantages would prevent her from writing or performing any other important activity. Woolf stresses the need for women to gain **economic independence** and **intellectual freedom**.

Woolf's rhetorical strategy is built on irony. She opens with a series of provocative statements: women are ignorant, they never made any great scientific or geographical discovery, they never had any ambitions of war and colonialism, they never wrote anything like Shakespeare's plays. What is there except for that? The answer is ironic and polemical: women have been busy giving birth to, taking care of, and nursing the millions of people now inhabiting the world. Without women, no historical or personal enterprise would have been possible.

Woolf's central metaphor is simple and effective: women could not write or create works of art as long as they were too busy working as wives, mothers or sisters, and as long as they did not have **a room of their own** — a place into which nobody could intrude. She stresses the necessity for women to gain economic independence and go on fighting for their freedom. Only then, maybe in a hundred years, Shakespeare's hypothetical sister, who was as gifted as her brother, will be able to be born again and write great poetry.

The importance of this essay has grown enormously since its publication, and it is now considered a **milestone of modern feminism** and women's studies.

## Shakespeare's Sister Will Be Born Some Day

This is a passage from *A Room of One's Own* in which Woolf directly addresses the young women in her audience. She begins with a list of facts: "You have never made a discovery of any sort of importance. You have never shaken an empire or led an army into battle. The plays of Shakespeare are not by you." These provocative words are not meant as an insult — they are Woolf's ironic way of pointing to the truth: women have never had the **opportunity** to do these things.

She then reminds her audience of how recent women's rights actually are. It was only in 1866 that the first two colleges for women were created in England. Only since 1880 was a married woman allowed by law to own her own property. And only since 1919 — "which is a whole nine years ago" — was she given a vote. At the time of writing, only about two thousand women were capable of earning over five hundred pounds a year. Given these facts, Woolf argues, we must agree that the lack of opportunity, training, encouragement, and money explains everything.

Then comes the most powerful moment. Woolf recalls **Shakespeare's sister** — the fictional character she had invented earlier in the essay. This imaginary sister was as gifted as her brother, but she never wrote a word. She died young and "lies buried where the omnibuses now stop, opposite the Elephant and Castle." She was never given the chance to use her talent.

But Woolf's message is not one of despair. She tells her audience that this poet who never wrote a word **still lives**: "She lives in you and in me, and in many other women who are not here tonight." If women work for her — if they gain economic independence, have a room of their own, and go on fighting for their freedom — then the opportunity will come, and "the dead poet who was Shakespeare's sister will put on the body which she has so often laid down." She will be born again, drawing her life from all the unknown women who came before her.

Woolf's final message is clear: women must prepare the way, so that one day a female Shakespeare can finally exist.
