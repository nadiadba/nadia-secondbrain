# James Joyce (1882–1941)

Together with T.S. Eliot, Virginia Woolf and Ezra Pound, **James Joyce** is one of the great writers of the literary revolution known as Modernism. Unlike Eliot and Pound, he was not English, and that is something worth considering when we talk about Modernism: not all of its major achievements came from the centre of the English-speaking world. Woolf was a woman, and that in itself made her an outsider at the time. Joyce was Irish and thus came from a country that in the very years in which he grew up was striving for its independence. The English language and culture were his and they were not, as he stated brilliantly in *Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*. Though Joyce was in many respects the archetypal Modernist writer — when the stream of consciousness technique is mentioned, his novel *Ulysses* immediately comes to mind — he began with a collection of short stories (*Dubliners*) written in what on the surface was a plain, realistic style, and his first published work was a book of simple musical poems, a far cry from any Modernist experiment.

## What Was Joyce's Irish Background?

James Joyce was born in Dublin in 1882 into a middle-class Catholic family, whose social status and financial resources gradually declined during his childhood. His father, John Stanislaus, had been an ardent supporter of Charles Parnell, the leader of the movement for Home Rule for Ireland, but after Parnell's death in 1891 he retired from politics and social life. He is portrayed as Simon Dedalus, a fallen father, in Joyce's first full-length novel, *Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man* (1916), where he is described as "a praiser of his own past." As such, he became representative for Joyce of the failures of his own country. From the age of six, Joyce attended two Jesuit schools, and like other writers educated by the Jesuits, his work shows the powerful intellectual and philosophical discipline he had received. In 1898 he entered University College, Dublin, to study modern languages. He graduated in 1902 and left for Paris to study medicine, returning to Dublin in April 1903 on news of his mother's illness (she died in August).

Despite being deeply rooted in Irish society and culture, from an early age Joyce was a rebel against what he thought was the pettiness of Dublin life. His rejection of the Catholic faith in the last year at his second Jesuit school, Belvedere College, was part of what he saw as being essential for him as a writer — to detach himself from everything that changed in Ireland.

!!! info "Joyce and the Cause of Irish Independence"
    The relationship between Joyce and Ireland is complex. On the surface, he seems to have rejected everything that he was; but, at a deeper level, though all of Joyce's works are centred on Ireland, and more specifically on the Dublin he knew, his self-imposed exile gave him the objectivity he needed to write material infused with the necessary emotional and intellectual detachment. Joyce's rejection of Irish public life began early: at school he refused to take part in the nationalistic activities of his fellow students, and he outraged his college debating society by praising the cosmopolitan European theatre while labelling the Irish Literary Theatre as lacking in artistic taste. When Easter Rising (celebrated by W.B. Yeats in a famous poem of the same title, in 1916) was brutally put down by the British, any political or public involvement in the cause of Irish independence was obviously what Joyce did not want for himself. In the short poem *Ireland, Island of Saints and Sages*, he documented the recurrent situation of Ireland: entrapment into marriage, family ties and politics, and that is exactly what Joyce felt he did not want — a distance between himself and his native country. The fact that he remained throughout his life all too Irish, though, is amply proved by his works.

## When and How Did Joyce Break Away from Ireland?

1904 was a turning point in Joyce's life. Already at University College he had decided to dedicate himself to art, and he refused to take part in the Celtic Revival or other forms of Irish artistic life. The main influence on him at this time was that of the Norwegian dramatist **Henrik Ibsen**, whose portraits of individuals suffocated by social norms and conventional morality strongly reflected Joyce's own experience of provincial Ireland. Finding life in Ireland an obstacle to his own artistic development — some of his early work had been refused publication by the university authorities — after graduating Joyce committed himself to a life of self-imposed exile. He went first to Paris, then to Pola and finally to Trieste. He was accompanied by **Nora Barnacle**, a woman from Galway whom he had become intimate on **16 June 1904** — the same day on which the story of his most famous work, *Ulysses*, takes place. Nora was not an intellectual, but she was witty and lively, a perfect counterpart to Joyce's more sombre character. They lived together for the rest of Joyce's life (they married in 1931) and had a son and a daughter.

## Were Joyce's Beginnings as a Writer Easy?

No, they were not. In fact, all of his books had a **troubled publication history**. By 1904 he had already started work on his first two important books: *Dubliners* (1914), a collection of short stories, and *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man* (1916), a semi-autobiographical novel, both of which he finished in Trieste. The fact that it took him ten years to get them published indicates that his career as a writer was not an easy one. In trying to have his books published, he met with three kinds of difficulty. First of all, his English and Irish publishers held up the publication of *Dubliners* because they objected to the book's language and content. But he also had to deal with **censorship**. *Ulysses* was actually banned in Britain and America on its publication in 1922, and it was not until 1933 that the ban was lifted in the USA. Finally, there was his own complexity as a writer; indeed, it took him sixteen years to finish *Finnegans Wake*, his enormously complex and ambitious last novel.

## How Did Joyce Become a Living Legend Among Artists and Writers?

When *Dubliners* came out in 1914, though it was not successful in commercial terms, it gained Joyce the admiration of critics and artists such as Ezra Pound, W.B. Yeats and T.S. Eliot. Pound was very active in connecting many intellectuals and patrons of the arts to find funds that would enable Joyce to complete his great novel, *Ulysses*, while he was working on it, from 1914 to 1921. Pound also convinced Joyce to leave Zurich, where he had fled with his family at the outbreak of World War I, and move to Paris in 1920. There Joyce was considered one of the **prophets of Modernism** and became a living legend among artists and writers. In fact, throughout the thirty-six years he lived in continental Europe Joyce was always the centre of a literary circle (in Trieste, for example, he was in close contact with **Italo Svevo**, whom he introduced to psychoanalysis). Despite his fame, Joyce was faced with constant financial difficulties throughout his life, but he was sustained and helped by writers and lovers of the arts from all over the world.

## What Were Joyce's Last Years Like?

In the intellectual and artistic milieu of Paris, *Ulysses* was published in 1922 and Joyce also set out to write his last novel, *Finnegans Wake*, on which he began work in 1923 and which was published in 1939. Although his reputation as a writer was firmly established by this time and his financial needs were met thanks to the will of generous and devoted friends, **Joyce's last years were not easy**. His daughter Lucia had a mental breakdown in 1932 and entered hospital in Switzerland the following year. Moreover, Joyce's health had always been poor, especially his eyesight: he experienced ever worse periods of blindness and in 1917 underwent the first of many **eye operations** against increasing blindness. In 1940, when France was occupied by the Germans, Joyce and his family returned to Zurich, where he died of a perforated ulcer on the operating table in 1941.

## Did Joyce Only Write Novels?

No, he didn't, though he is obviously most famous for his novels. One of his first works was a play, *Exiles* (1918), which was not very successful, but in its interesting results on the themes of Joyce's own life, exile. More interesting are Joyce's books of poetry. The first, *Chamber Music* (1907), was in fact his first published work: the title points to the musical quality of the poems and indeed they have been given musical settings by a number of musicians. In 1927 Pomes Penyeach came out, consisting of a few lyrics based on elementary contrasts that recall Blake's *Songs of Innocence and Experience*. When Joyce brought these two volumes together in *Collected Poems* (1936), he only added one short poem: *Ecce Puer*. It was written in 1932 on the occasion of his grandson's birth soon after his father's death. It is worth quoting it here since it shows the **touching simplicity of Joyce's verse**, as opposed to the difficulties of his prose.

!!! quote "Ecce Puer"
    Of the dark past / A child is born; / With joy and grief / My heart is torn.

    Calm in his cradle / The living lies. / May love and mercy / Unclose his eyes!

    Young life is breathed / On the glass; / The world that was not / Comes to pass.

    A child is sleeping: / An old man gone. / O, father forsaken, / Forgive your son!

## Why Is It Essential to Have an Idea of All of Joyce's Works in Order to Understand Him as a Writer and a Man?

Joyce's major works are related in a way that is unusual with respect to most writers. In the first place, they have only **one setting**: early 20th-century Dublin, with its people, streets, houses and language, precisely every page written by Joyce, who reproduces them with great attention to precise details. Dublin was for him, as he said, the "emphasis," or mould (that is, the matrix) of the world. Secondly, characters from the Dublin reappear: in *Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man* and in *Ulysses*; in fact, a day in the life of Leopold Bloom, the main story of *Ulysses*, was originally meant to be included in *Dubliners*. And Stephen Dedalus, the protagonist of *A Portrait*, opens Joyce's next novel, *Ulysses* (the first three chapters are centred on him), interacting with Bloom throughout the story. Thirdly, Joyce's novels show a **shift from the particular to the universal**. The journey of his narrative was from small to large, from the contained short stories of *Dubliners* to the autobiographical novel *A Portrait*, to that great picture of Dublin life that is *Ulysses*. There was also a movement in the shift from the lyrical style of *A Portrait* (which in fact contains some lyrics) to the epic style of *Ulysses*, modelled on Homer's *Odyssey*, as the title implies.

## Dubliners (1914)

### What Kind of Book Is Dubliners?

*Dubliners* is a collection of **fifteen short stories**, and in each of them the failure of self-realisation of an inhabitant of Dublin is examined in biographical and psychological detail. It is Joyce's first great portrait of Dublin and, as he later stated, "My intention was to write a chapter of the moral history of my country and I chose Dublin for the scene because that city seemed to me the centre of paralysis."

This **"paralysis"** — the paralysis of will, courage and self-knowledge — is what leads ordinary men and women to accept the limitations imposed by the social context they live in. The sense of paralysis and of stagnation runs through all fifteen stories and is already clear in the first, "The Sisters," which is about an old and failing priest who dies after becoming paralysed. The child through whose eyes the story is told is both fascinated and horrified by the strange and, to him, incomprehensible word he hears whispered: "paralysis." Joyce arranged the stories so that they should best present his idea of paralysis in **four stages**: **childhood**, **youth**, **maturity** and **public life**. The final story, "The Dead," sums them all up. The shift in Joyce's art and life from the particular to the universal, from the personal to the objective, is mirrored in the progression of the stories themselves: the first three being in the first person and the rest in the third person.

### Why Did It Take So Long to Get Dubliners Published?

Twelve of the stories of *Dubliners* had been written by 1905, when Joyce was living in Trieste. Although the manuscript was accepted by a publisher the following year, the book did not appear in print until 1914 because of objections raised by his printers (in the end, the book was submitted 18 times to a total of 15 publishers). Meanwhile, Joyce had added three more stories, the last and longest, "The Dead," was finished in 1907.

Thus, for nearly ten years Joyce fought against publishers who considered *Dubliners* **"immoral"** for its pitiless portrait of Irish city life. In the end he won, and the final published text included the features that were thought objectionable — the repetition of "bloody," a hint at King Edward VII and, what was most offensive to the Irish publishers, the mention of real places and people in Dublin. Joyce's almost total refusal to accept any changes to the original text during his long struggle with the publishers might seem obstinate, but his most urgent need as a writer was **to tell the truth as he saw it**. That for him meant keeping away from the contemporary tide of triumphant and sentimentalised Irish nationalism, as he saw it, which distorted the reality of Irish life.

### Is Dubliners a Modernist Work?

No. *Dubliners* is not a Modernist work if by that we mean a total break with tradition and a revolutionary use of language and syntax. In fact, it partakes of the two modes of writing prevalent at the turn of the 20th century: **Realism** and **Symbolism**.

The style of *Dubliners*, however, is complex. It is **seemingly realistic** in that it perfectly recreates characters, places, streets, pubs and idioms of contemporary Dublin. On the other hand, Joyce makes use of a subtle **symbolic effect** which gives the common object unexpected depth and becomes the key to a new, more conscious view of reality. Joyce himself coined the definition **"epiphany"** — gradually concerned as "manifestation," "showing" — e.g. in the showing of the Christ child to the Magi — to indicate that moment when a simple object, fact or an ordinary situation suddenly explodes with meaning and makes a person realise something (usually unpleasant) about themselves. As would later occur in *Ulysses* with the Homeric epic, here the contrast between the aspirations that dominate the Map and the trivial aspirations that take place on the streets of Dublin is bitterly ironic. In fact, the epiphanies in *Dubliners* do not so much lead to a revelation, but rather reveal comfortable, accepted truths as fallacies.

### Is the Style of Dubliners Typical of Short-Story Writing?

No, it isn't. The older technique of short-story writing (as in the works of Edgar Allan Poe, Henry James or the nineteenth-century French writers) tried to give the stories unexpected turns, in the form of surprises and coincidences, that would make daily life more eventful in order to make up for the reduced plot inclusion of short stories as compared to novels. Instead, Joyce followed the path opened by the Russian author Anton Chekhov, and his stories calmly seem to adapt to the flow of daily life. They begin in the middle of something and stop without an apparent conclusion. As the critic Gary Leonard has noted, they read "as if someone has made a two-hour film by pointing the camera at a tripod and letting it run, and then brought the result directly to the screen, with no editing." Nothing particularly remarkable happens in them, and yet the reader is taken through a maze of obscure street names and a stray selection of ordinary objects, such as a gold coin, a lost plum cake or a confiscated adventure book. The fact that so little happens is of course related to the main theme of the collection, i.e. the **paralysis** that envelops the routine lives of the city's inhabitants. Behind the apparent routine of those lives, however, the most pressing issues of contemporary Irish life are clearly present, such as Ireland's difficult relations with **Britain**, the tensions of **family ties**, and the strong presence of **religion** in Irish life.

## Eveline — James Joyce, *Dubliners* (1914)

### Introduction

"Eveline" belongs to the group of short stories dealing with **youth** in *Dubliners*. In Dublin's stagnant life, however, youth is not synonymous with vitality and enthusiasm. Quite the contrary: the beginning of the story is typical — the day is dying, the young woman is watching the world go by under her window as if she were an old person confined to her home, and the narrator says: "She was tired." Joyce implies her **tiredness with the life she is living** rather than actual physical fatigue. As with the other stories of the youth group, this one too has as its centre a love story — or rather a story of escape: Eveline has agreed to run away with a violent and alcoholic father and a miserable home to follow **Frank**, a sailor, to Buenos Aires. However, in the end she does not have the courage to go aboard the ship. She just stands on the quay, unable to move, as if paralysed.

### Summary and Analysis

Eveline sits at the window watching the evening invade the avenue. Her head is leaned against the curtains and in her nostrils is "the odour of dusty cretonne." She remembers the past: the field where children used to play, the families that have now gone away. Her mother is dead, her brother Ernest is dead too. Everything changes — and now she is going to go away like the others, to **leave her home**.

She looks around the room, reviewing the familiar objects she has dusted once a week for so many years. She has never even found out the name of the priest whose yellowing photograph hangs on the wall. She thinks about her work at the Stores, about Miss Gavan, about whether people would say she was a fool to run away.

Then she thinks of **Frank**. He is very kind, manly, open-hearted. He has a home waiting for her in Buenos Aires. She remembers how they met, how he took her to see *The Bohemian Girl*, how he sang about "the lass that loves a sailor." He had started as a deck boy and had sailed through the Straits of Magellan.

But her **father found out** about the affair and had forbidden her to see Frank. At the same time, Eveline remembers her father's violence and his control over the family money — she had to hand over her entire wages and Harry's too. She had to work hard to keep the home together and see that the children went to school and got their meals. It was a hard life — and yet, now that she is about to leave it, she does not find it a wholly undesirable life.

The turning point comes when she remembers the **promise she made to her dying mother**: to keep the home together as long as she could. She hears again her mother's final crazy words and feels terror. She must escape! Frank would save her. He would give her life, perhaps love too.

At the station, among the swaying crowd, Frank holds her hand. The boat blows a long mournful whistle into the mist. But at the last moment, **she cannot go**. All the seas of the world tumble about her heart. She grips the iron railing with both hands. Frank calls to her — "Come!" — but she sets her white face to him, passive, like a helpless animal. **Her eyes give him no sign of love or farewell or recognition.** She is paralysed.

Joyce shows that Eveline does not make a free choice. She is crushed between her desire for escape and her sense of duty. The promise to her mother acts like a chain. Fear of the unknown becomes a form of **paralysis** — the central theme of the whole collection.
