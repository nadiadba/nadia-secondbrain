# James Joyce (1882–1941)

## Life and Works

James Joyce is one of the great writers of **Modernism**, together with T.S. Eliot, Virginia Woolf and Ezra Pound. Unlike the others, Joyce was Irish — he came from a country that was still fighting for its independence from Britain, and this shaped all of his work.

He was born in Dublin in 1882 into a middle-class Catholic family whose social status and financial resources gradually declined during his childhood. His father, John Stanislaus, had supported Charles Parnell, the leader of the Home Rule movement, but after Parnell's death in 1891 he retired from politics and social life. Joyce later described him in *Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man* (1916) as "a praiser of his own past." From the age of six, Joyce attended two Jesuit schools, whose strict intellectual training deeply influenced his writing. In 1898 he entered University College, Dublin, to study modern languages.

Even though he grew up in Irish culture, Joyce was a rebel from a young age. He rejected the Catholic faith and refused to take part in the Celtic Revival or other forms of Irish artistic life. He was strongly influenced by the Norwegian writer Henrik Ibsen, whose portraits of individuals trapped by social norms reflected Joyce's own experience of provincial Ireland. Joyce's relationship with his country was complex: he rejected Irish public life and nationalism, yet all of his works are set in Dublin. Living abroad gave him the distance he needed to write about his city honestly.

In **1904** Joyce met **Nora Barnacle**, a woman from Galway, on **16 June** — the same day on which the story of *Ulysses* takes place. They left Ireland together and lived in Trieste, Paris and Zurich. Joyce chose a life of exile, but he never stopped writing about Dublin.

His career was marked by a **troubled publication history**. *Dubliners* was rejected 18 times by 15 publishers before being published in 1914, because publishers considered it "immoral" for its honest portrait of Irish life. *Ulysses* was banned in Britain and America from 1922 until 1933. His last novel, *Finnegans Wake*, took him sixteen years to finish.

After *Dubliners* came out, Ezra Pound and T.S. Eliot helped Joyce find financial support. By the 1920s he was seen as one of the **prophets of Modernism**. In Trieste he became the centre of a literary circle and grew close to **Italo Svevo**, the Italian writer, whom he introduced to psychoanalysis. Joyce also wrote poetry: *Chamber Music* (1907) and the moving poem *Ecce Puer* (1932), written when his grandson was born, shortly after his own father died.

Joyce's last years were not easy. His daughter Lucia had a mental breakdown in 1932, and Joyce himself suffered from serious eye problems, going through many operations against increasing blindness. In 1940, when France was occupied by the Nazis, he moved to Zurich, where he died in January 1941.

All of Joyce's works are connected: they share the same setting — Dublin — and the same characters appear in different books. His writing moved **from the particular to the universal**, from the short stories of *Dubliners* to the epic scale of *Ulysses*, which is based on Homer's *Odyssey*.

## Dubliners (1914)

*Dubliners* is a collection of **fifteen short stories**. In each of them, the failure of self-realisation of an inhabitant of Dublin is examined in biographical and psychological detail. As Joyce wrote, "My intention was to write a chapter of the moral history of my country and I chose Dublin for the scene because that city seemed to me the centre of paralysis."

**"Paralysis"** — the inability to act, to find courage, to know oneself — is the central theme of the whole book. It leads ordinary people to accept the limitations imposed by the social context they live in. Joyce organised the stories in **four stages**: **childhood**, **youth**, **maturity** and **public life**. The last story, "The Dead," brings them all together. The point of view also changes: the first three stories are told in the first person, the rest in the third person.

*Dubliners* is not fully Modernist — it uses both **Realism** and **Symbolism**. On one hand, it describes characters, streets, pubs and ways of speaking in Dublin with great accuracy. On the other hand, Joyce uses a **symbolic technique**: ordinary objects suddenly gain a deeper meaning through what he called an **"epiphany"** — a moment when something simple makes a person realise an uncomfortable truth about themselves. These epiphanies are never happy discoveries; they reveal comfortable, accepted truths as fallacies.

Joyce's style is also different from traditional short stories. Writers like Poe or Henry James used surprises and plot twists. Joyce, like the Russian writer Chekhov, followed the natural flow of everyday life: his stories begin in the middle of something and end without a clear conclusion. Nothing dramatic happens, yet behind the routine of ordinary lives, the pressing issues of contemporary Irish life are clearly present: Ireland's difficult relations with Britain, the tensions of family ties, and the strong presence of religion.

## Eveline

"Eveline" is the fourth story in *Dubliners* and belongs to the **youth** group. But in Dublin, youth does not mean energy or hope: the story opens at the end of the day, with Eveline sitting at her window, watching the world outside. She is tired — not in her body, but tired of the life she lives. Joyce implies her **tiredness with the life she is living** rather than actual physical fatigue.

Eveline has agreed to leave Dublin with **Frank**, a kind sailor who wants to take her to Buenos Aires. As she sits at the window, she looks around her room at the familiar objects she has dusted once a week for so many years, wondering where on earth all the dust comes from. She thinks she will never see those objects again, and yet she has never even found out the name of the priest whose yellowing photograph hangs on the wall.

Her life has been difficult. Her mother is dead, her brother Ernest is dead too, and the Waters have gone back to England. Her father is violent, drinks, and controls the family money — Eveline must hand over her entire wages and her brother Harry's too. She works hard to keep the house together, making sure the two younger children go to school and get their meals regularly. It is a hard life, and yet now that she is about to leave it, she does not find it a wholly undesirable life.

Then she thinks of **Frank**. He is very kind, manly, open-hearted. He has a home waiting for her in Buenos Aires. She remembers how they met, how he took her to see *The Bohemian Girl* at the theatre, how he sang about "the lass that loves a sailor." He had started as a deck boy and had sailed through the Straits of Magellan. Frank represents her chance to escape — but her father found out about the affair and forbade her to see him.

The turning point comes when she remembers the **promise she made to her dying mother**: to keep the family together as long as she could. She hears again her mother's last words, and a feeling of terror rises in her. She must escape! Frank would save her. He would give her life, perhaps love too.

At the dock, among the crowd, Frank holds her hand. The boat blows a long mournful whistle into the mist. But at the last moment, Eveline **cannot move**. All the seas of the world tumble about her heart. She grips the iron railing with both hands, her face white, like a helpless animal. Frank calls "Come!" — but her eyes show him no sign of love, farewell or recognition. She is **paralysed**.

Joyce shows that Eveline is trapped between two forces: her desire for a new life and her sense of duty. The promise to her mother acts like a chain, and the fear of the unknown becomes paralysis — the same paralysis that runs through every story in the collection.
