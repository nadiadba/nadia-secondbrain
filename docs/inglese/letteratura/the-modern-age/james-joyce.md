# James Joyce

## Life and Background

James Joyce (1882–1941) is one of the great writers of the literary revolution known as Modernism. Together with T.S. Eliot, Virginia Woolf and Ezra Pound, he changed the way novels and stories are written. Unlike Eliot and Pound, Joyce was not English, and this is something worth considering: not all of Modernism's major achievements came from the centre of the English-speaking world. Joyce was Irish, and he came from a country that was still fighting for its independence from Britain.

Joyce was born in Dublin in 1882 into a middle-class Catholic family, but his family's social status and financial resources gradually declined during his childhood. His father, John Stanislaus, had been a supporter of Charles Parnell, the leader of the movement for Home Rule for Ireland. After Parnell's death in 1891, John retired from politics and social life. Joyce later portrayed him as Simon Dedalus in his first full-length novel, *Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man* (1916), where he described him as "a praiser of his own past."

From the age of six, Joyce attended two Jesuit schools, and like other writers educated by the Jesuits, his work shows the powerful intellectual and philosophical training he received. In 1898 he entered University College, Dublin, to study modern languages. He graduated in 1902 and left for Paris to study medicine, but returned to Dublin in April 1903 when his mother became ill (she died soon after).

### Breaking Away from Ireland

Despite being deeply rooted in Irish society and culture, Joyce was a rebel from an early age. He rejected the Catholic faith, which he saw as part of what held Ireland back. His rejection of Catholicism, his refusal to take part in the Celtic Revival or other forms of Irish artistic life, and his interest in the Norwegian dramatist Henrik Ibsen — whose portraits of individuals trapped by social norms reflected Joyce's own experience of provincial Ireland — all pushed him to leave.

In **1904**, Joyce met **Nora Barnacle**, a woman from Galway whom he had become close to on **16 June 1904** — the same day on which the story of his most famous work, *Ulysses*, takes place. Nora was not an intellectual, but she was witty and lively. They left Ireland together and lived first in Trieste, then in Paris, and finally in Zurich. They married in 1931 and had a son and a daughter.

### A Troubled Publication History

Joyce's beginnings as a writer were not easy. He had to deal with **censorship** throughout his career. His publishers objected to the language and content of *Dubliners*, which they thought was too honest about Irish life. The book was submitted 18 times to a total of 15 publishers before it was finally published in 1914. *Ulysses* was actually banned in Britain and America on its publication in 1922, and it was not until 1933 that the ban was lifted in the USA. It took him sixteen years to finish *Finnegans Wake*, his enormously complex and ambitious last novel.

### Joyce's Last Years

After *Dubliners* came out in 1914, Ezra Pound and T.S. Eliot admired Joyce and helped him find support. Pound was very active in connecting Joyce to intellectuals and patrons of the arts who could fund his work. By the 1920s, Joyce was considered one of the **prophets of Modernism** and became a living legend among artists and writers.

In Trieste, Joyce was the centre of a literary circle. He was in close contact with **Italo Svevo**, whom he introduced to psychoanalysis. Despite his fame, Joyce always faced financial difficulties, and he was supported and helped by writers and lovers of the arts from all over the world.

Joyce's last years were not easy. His daughter Lucia had a mental breakdown in 1932 and entered hospital in Switzerland. Moreover, Joyce's health had always been poor: he suffered from a serious eye condition and had to go through many **eye operations** against increasing blindness. In 1940, when France was occupied by the Germans, Joyce and his family returned to Zurich, where he died of a perforated ulcer on 13 January 1941.

## Joyce's Main Themes

Joyce's work is built around a few central themes that come back again and again:

- **Ireland**: Joyce always wrote about Dublin, even though he lived abroad. He wanted to show the reality of Irish life honestly, without the romantic nationalism that many Irish writers used at the time.
- **The reworking of myth**: especially in *Ulysses*, Joyce takes ancient stories (like Homer's *Odyssey*) and places them in modern Dublin.
- **Parent-children relationships**: the tension between parents and children, duty and freedom, appears in almost every work.
- **Love and death**: Joyce explores how love can be both a source of life and a kind of prison, and how death shapes the way we see the people around us.
- **Female consciousness**: Joyce was interested in how women think and feel, and he gave female characters rich inner lives — unusual for writers of his time.

## Joyce's Style: The Epiphany

One of the most important ideas in Joyce's writing is the concept of **epiphany**. Joyce himself defined it as a "manifestation" or "showing": a moment when a simple object, fact or ordinary situation suddenly explodes with meaning and makes a person realise something they could not see before. These moments are not dramatic — they are quiet, almost invisible. A letter, a street, a look from a stranger can trigger an epiphany.

In *Dubliners*, the epiphanies do not lead to a revelation that changes everything. They lead to a realisation that is often uncomfortable and accepted, rather than fought against. The characters understand something painful about their lives, but they cannot do anything about it.

## Dubliners (1914)

### What Kind of Book Is It?

*Dubliners* is a collection of **fifteen short stories**, and in each of them the failure of self-realisation of an inhabitant of Dublin is examined in biographical and psychological detail. It is Joyce's first great portrait of Dublin and, as he later stated, "My intention was to write a chapter of the moral history of my country and I chose Dublin for the scene because that city seemed to me the centre of paralysis."

This **paralysis** — the paralysis of will, courage and self-knowledge — is what leads ordinary men and women to accept the limitations imposed by the social context they live in. The sense of paralysis and of stagnation runs through all fifteen stories and is already clear in the first, "The Sisters."

### Structure

Joyce arranged the stories in a strict order. They present his idea of paralysis in **four stages**:

1. **Childhood** — the first three stories
2. **Youth** — stories about young people making their first choices
3. **Maturity** — stories about adults trapped in routine
4. **Public life** — the final story, "The Dead," which sums them all up

The shift in the progression of the stories is also reflected in the narrative voice: the first three stories are told in the first person, and the rest in the third person.

### Publication Difficulties

Twelve of the stories of *Dubliners* had been written by 1905, when Joyce was living in Trieste. But the manuscript was accepted by a publisher the following year, the book did not appear in print until 1914 because of objections raised by his printers. In the end, the book was submitted 18 times to a total of 15 publishers. Meanwhile, Joyce had added three more stories, the last and longest, "The Dead," was finished in 1907.

For nearly ten years Joyce fought against publishers who considered *Dubliners* "immoral" for its realistic portrait of Irish city life. The objections included the repetition of the word "bloody," references to King Edward VII, and — most importantly — the mention of real places and people in Dublin. Joyce almost totally refused to accept any changes. His most urgent need as a writer was to **tell the truth as he saw it**, even if that meant keeping away from the romantic image of triumphant Irish nationalism.

### Is Dubliners a Modernist Work?

*Dubliners* is not a Modernist work if by that we mean a total break with tradition and a revolutionary use of language and syntax. In fact, it takes part in the two modes of writing that were popular at the turn of the 20th century: **Realism** and **Symbolism**.

The style of *Dubliners* is complex. It is **seemingly realistic** in that it perfectly recreates characters, places, streets, pubs and idioms of contemporary Dublin. On the other hand, Joyce makes use of a subtle **symbolic effect** which gives the common object unexpected depth: ordinary things become the key to a more complete, more conscious view of reality.

### The Style of Dubliners

The older technique of short-story writing (as in the works of Edgar Allan Poe or Henry James) tried to give the stories unexpected turns, in the form of surprises and coincidences. Joyce followed a different path, closer to the Russian writer Anton Chekhov: his stories seem to adapt to the flow of daily life. They begin in the middle of something and stop without an apparent conclusion.

As the critic Gary Leonard has noted, they read "as if someone has made a two-hour film by pointing the camera at a tripod and letting it run, and then brought the result directly to the screen, with no editing." Nothing particularly remarkable happens in them, and yet the reader is taken through a maze of obscure street names, ordinary objects, and small episodes that slowly reveal the routine lives of the city's inhabitants.

### Main Themes of the Collection

Behind the apparent routine of those lives, the most pressing issues of contemporary Irish life are clearly present:

- Ireland's difficult relations with **Britain**
- The tensions of **family ties**
- The strong presence of **religion** in Irish life
- The **paralysis** that prevents people from changing their situation

## Eveline: A Story About a Decision That Cannot Be Made

### Introduction

"Eveline" belongs to the group of short stories dealing with youth in *Dubliners*. It is the fourth story in the collection. In Dublin's stagnant life, however, youth is not synonymous with vitality and enthusiasm. Quite the contrary: the beginning of the story is typical — the day is dying, and the young woman is watching the world go by under her window (as if she were an old person confined to her home). As the narrator says, "She was tired."

Like the other stories of the youth group, this one too has as its centre a love story — or rather, a story of escape: Eveline has agreed to run away with a violent and alcoholic father and a miserable home, to follow **Frank**, a sailor, to Buenos Aires. However, again as in the other youth group stories, the protagonist is not able to escape from the **deadness** of everyday life. In the end, she does not have the courage to go aboard the ship that would take her and Frank to Buenos Aires. She just stands on the quay, unable to move, as if paralysed.

### The Story

Eveline sits at the window watching the evening invade the avenue. Her head is leaned against the window curtains and in her nostrils is "the odour of dusty cretonne." She is tired.

She looks outside and remembers the past. Few people pass. She hears the noise of footsteps on the pavement. She thinks of the field where children used to play — the Devines, the Waters, the Dunns, little Keogh the cripple — and of her brothers and sisters. But things have changed. Her mother is dead, her brother Ernest is dead too, and the Waters have gone back to England.

Now everything changes. She is going to go away like the others, to **leave her home**. She looks around the room, reviewing all the familiar objects she has "dusted once a week for so many years," wondering where on earth all the dust comes from. She thinks she will never see those familiar objects again, and yet she had never even found out the name of the priest whose yellowing photograph hangs on the wall, beside the picture of the promises made to Blessed Margaret Mary Alacoque.

### Frank and the Promise of Escape

Then she thinks of Frank. He is very kind, manly, open-hearted. She is to go away with him by the night boat to be his wife and to live with him in **Buenos Aires**, where he has a home waiting for her.

She remembers the first time she met him: he was lodging in a house on the main road where she used to visit. That was only a few weeks ago. He had a peaked cap pushed back on his head and his hair tumbled forward over a face of bronze. Then they got to know each other. He used to meet her outside the Stores every evening and see her home. He took her to see *The Bohemian Girl* and she felt happy, seated in an "unaccustomed part of the theatre" with him. He was awfully fond of music and sang a little. People knew they were going together and, when he sang about the lass that loves a sailor, she always felt pleasantly confused.

He used to call her "Poppens" out of fun. First of all it had been an excitement for her to have a fellow. He had started as a deck boy "at a pound a month" on a ship of the Allan Line going out to Canada. He told her the names of the ships he had been on and the names of the different services. He had sailed through the Straits of Magellan and he told her stories of the terrible Patagonians.

### The Inner Conflict

But her father found out about the affair and had forbidden her to have anything to do with Frank. Despite this, she must escape. She thinks about her father's violence. He used to give her the money she earned — seven shillings — and Harry's money too, and say what he would do to her "only for her dead mother's sake." She had to work hard to keep the house together, to see that the two young children went to school regularly and got their meals. It was hard work — a hard life — and now that she was about to leave it, she did not find it a wholly undesirable life.

She remembers her **promise to her dying mother**: to keep the home together as long as she could. She remembers her mother's last words, the final crazy night of her illness, and feels the pitiful vision of her mother's life closing in on her.

### The Ending: Paralysis at the Dock

She stands up in a sudden impulse of terror. She must escape! Frank would save her. He would give her life, perhaps love, too. She wants to live.

She stands among the swaying crowd at the station. Frank holds her hand. The boat is there, blowing its long mournful whistle into the mist. She hears Frank's voice urging her to come.

But at the last moment, **she cannot go**. All the seas of the world tumble about her heart. A bell clangs upon her heart. She grips the iron railing with both hands. She sends "a cry of anguish" — but she does not move.

Frank calls to her: "Come!" — but she sets her white face to him, passive, like a helpless animal. **Her eyes give him no sign of love or farewell or recognition.** She is paralysed.

### Analysis: Why Eveline Cannot Leave

Joyce wants us to understand that Eveline does not make a free choice. She is crushed by two opposing forces:

- Her **desire for a different life** — Frank offers her love, a new home, escape from her violent father
- Her **sense of duty and obligation** — the promise she made to her dying mother to keep the family together, her fear of the unknown

The **promise to her mother** acts like a chain. It holds Eveline to the dusty house, to the hard work, to the father who drinks and sometimes hits her. At the same time, the world outside Ireland is uncertain and frightening. Buenos Aires is far away, and Eveline has never left Dublin.

Joyce shows how **fear can become a kind of paralysis**. Eveline is not stopped by a physical barrier — no one blocks her way to the ship. The barrier is inside her mind. The familiar life, even if painful, feels safer than the unknown.

Her "epiphany" is not a happy one. She realises, in that terrible moment at the dock, that she is **unable to keep her promise and unable to escape**. She is trapped between two worlds, and she chooses — or rather, she fails to choose — the one she already knows.

The last image of the story is powerful: Eveline stands still, gripping the iron railing, her face white and expressionless, like "a helpless animal." She gives Frank no sign of love, farewell or recognition. This is Joyce's way of showing that paralysis has taken over completely — Eveline is no longer a person making a decision, but someone who has been defeated by the forces around her.
