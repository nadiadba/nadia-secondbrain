# James Joyce

James Joyce (1882–1941) is often considered the writer who most clearly captured the voice of Dublin at the turn of the 20th century. He was Irish, but he spent most of his adult life abroad: in Trieste, Paris, and Zurich. Even so, almost every page he wrote returns to his native city. Joyce’s style changes a lot from book to book, but his concern always remains the same: the difficulty of living honestly in a world full of small compromises.

## A longer look at Joyce’s life and style

Joyce was born into a Catholic family north of Dublin. His childhood was not easy: his father drank too much, and the family moved often because of money troubles. These early experiences left a deep mark on him. After school he studied modern languages, then fell in love with Nora Barnacle, who would become his wife and a model for several of his female characters.

The first books he published were not well received. Publishers thought he was too bold and the language too plain. He believed in writing in a way that sounded like real people talking — the places where people are afraid to say exactly what they feel. For this reason, his early work is full of everyday details: the smell of tea, the exact way a door creaks, the rhythm of a mother’s voice.

Joyce also introduced the idea of the **epiphany**. For him, an epiphany is a small moment when a character suddenly sees their life clearly. It is not always happy. Often it is a shocking or painful realization. In *Dubliners*, these moments are the heart of the stories.

## Dubliners: a book about Dublin and small lives

*Dubliners* is a collection of fifteen short stories that Joyce published in 1914. He worked on it for almost ten years, and many publishers rejected it because they considered it too honest about Irish priests and poor people. The final book is structured in a very precise way: it begins with children, continues with young adults, and ends with older people. In this sense, it resembles a single day, from morning to night.

At the beginning, we meet children who play in schoolyards and are curious about the world. The stories show how innocence quickly disappears. As the collection continues, the characters become adults who have to make decisions about work, love and duty. Near the end, the characters are older, quieter, and often alone. Joyce paints Dublin as a city where people are trapped in their routines and where it is difficult to escape.

Each story works like a small window into the life of one person. Joyce is not interested in big historical events; he focuses on what people think and feel at a particular moment. In *Dubliners*, the ordinary becomes important. A letter, a train journey, a dance can change everything for a character.

### How the stories are arranged

The order of the stories is important, because it creates an emotional journey. The first stories are about boys: *The Sisters*, *An Encounter* and *Araby*. Then come stories about teenagers and young adults, including *Eveline*, *After the Race*, *Two Gallants*, and *The Boarding House*. Later, Joyce presents stories about marriage, work and loneliness: *A Little Cloud*, *Counterparts*, *Clay*, *A Painful Case*. The last stories, like *Ivy Day in the Committee Room* and *The Dead*, offer a quieter, reflective tone. In *The Dead*, the collection ends with an epiphany that is both sad and beautiful.

Because Joyce arranges the stories like this, *Dubliners* can be read as a progression from childhood to old age. The city remains the same, but the people change. The themes of paralysis and escape appear again and again, showing that this is not only a problem of one person, but a problem of Dublin itself.

## Eveline: a story about a decision that cannot be made

“Eveline” is the fourth story in *Dubliners*. It is a short, simple story, but it contains everything that Joyce loves: memory, duty, fear, and a single moment that changes the character forever.

Eveline is a young woman who lives with her father and two younger children in a small, dark house. Their mother died when Eveline was a child. The father drinks and sometimes hits them. The house is described as dusty and still, with curtains that do not open. Eveline spends her days doing household tasks: cleaning, making tea, ironing. In her mind she carries a promise she made to her mother: to keep the family together and to take care of the children. This promise is like a chain that holds her to the house.

Beyond the house, the city of Dublin is also a prison. Eveline walks to the shop, the church, the station, but everything is familiar and unchanged. She thinks of the noise of the cars, of the river, of the people who pass by without noticing her. The story often returns to simple images: the brown wainscoting, the light through the window, the sound of the street at night. In these images, Joyce shows how Eveline feels trapped.

Then there is Frank. He is a sailor who has promised Eveline a new life in Buenos Aires. He writes letters and speaks to her warmly. He speaks of leaving the country, of adventure, of love. For the first time, Eveline sees a possibility of escape from her small, suffocating world.

The most important part of the story happens in Eveline’s head. Joyce writes her thoughts with a style that is almost like free indirect discourse: the narrator knows what Eveline thinks, but the sentences are written in her voice. She remembers her mother’s last moments in the kitchen, the smell of the tea, and the words her mother said about duty. She thinks about the promise, about Frank, about the word “home.”

When Eveline finally goes to the dock with Frank, the sea is in front of her for the first time. The wind and the smell of salt are new. The boat is ready, the gangway is lowered. Men shout. The boat heaves with the waves. Eveline feels the movement inside her as if she were in a dream.

At the last moment, something blocks her. It is not a physical barrier, but an inner one. She hears Frank calling “Eveline!” and the voice seems both close and very far away. In that moment she remembers her promise, and the image of her father’s face, and the dusty curtains of her house. She remains rooted to the spot. The story ends with Eveline standing still, unable to move, while the boat slowly leaves without her.

### Why Eveline cannot go

Joyce wants us to understand that Eveline does not choose freely. She is crushed by two forces: her desire for a different life, and her feeling of obligation. The promise to her mother acts like a weight. At the same time, the world outside is uncertain and frightening. Joyce shows how fear can become a kind of paralysis.

In the end, Eveline’s “epiphany” is not a happy one. She realizes that she is unable to keep her promise and unable to escape. This realization is the most painful moment of the story, and it is also the moment when the reader understands everything about her.
