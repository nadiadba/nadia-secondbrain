# James Joyce (1882–1941)

## Life and Works

James Joyce is one of the great writers of the literary revolution known as Modernism, together with T.S. Eliot, Virginia Woolf and Ezra Pound. Unlike the others, Joyce was Irish — he came from a country that was still striving for its independence from Britain, and this shaped his entire work.

He was born in Dublin in 1882 into a middle-class Catholic family whose social status gradually declined during his childhood. His father, John Stanislaus, had been a supporter of Charles Parnell, the leader of the Home Rule movement, but after Parnell's death in 1891 he retired from politics and social life. Joyce later portrayed him as Simon Dedalus in *Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man* (1916), describing him as "a praiser of his own past." From the age of six, Joyce attended two Jesuit schools, whose intellectual and philosophical discipline deeply influenced his work. In 1898 he entered University College, Dublin, to study modern languages.

Despite being rooted in Irish culture, Joyce was a rebel from an early age. He rejected the Catholic faith and refused to take part in the Celtic Revival or other forms of Irish artistic life. He was deeply influenced by the Norwegian dramatist Henrik Ibsen, whose portraits of individuals suffocated by social norms reflected Joyce's own experience of provincial Ireland. Joyce's relationship with Ireland was complex: he rejected Irish public life and nationalism, yet all of his works are centred on Dublin. His self-imposed exile gave him the objectivity and emotional detachment he needed to write about his native city honestly.

In **1904** Joyce met **Nora Barnacle**, a woman from Galway, on **16 June** — the same day on which the story of *Ulysses* takes place. They left Ireland together and lived in Trieste, Paris and Zurich. Joyce committed himself to a life of exile, but he never stopped writing about Dublin.

His career as a writer was marked by a **troubled publication history**. *Dubliners* was submitted 18 times to 15 publishers before being published in 1914, because publishers considered it "immoral" for its honest portrait of Irish life. *Ulysses* was banned in Britain and America from 1922 until 1933. It took him sixteen years to finish *Finnegans Wake*, his last and most complex novel.

After *Dubliners* came out, Ezra Pound and T.S. Eliot helped Joyce find support, and by the 1920s he was considered one of the **prophets of Modernism**. In Trieste he was the centre of a literary circle and was in close contact with **Italo Svevo**, whom he introduced to psychoanalysis. Joyce also wrote poetry: *Chamber Music* (1907) and the touching poem *Ecce Puer* (1932), written for his grandson's birth soon after his father's death.

Joyce's last years were not easy. His daughter Lucia had a mental breakdown in 1932, and Joyce himself suffered from serious eye problems, undergoing many operations against increasing blindness. In 1940, when France was occupied by the Germans, he moved to Zurich, where he died of a perforated ulcer in January 1941.

All of Joyce's works are connected: they share the same setting — early 20th-century Dublin — and the same characters reappear across different books. His narrative journey went **from the particular to the universal**, from the contained short stories of *Dubliners* to the epic scale of *Ulysses*, modelled on Homer's *Odyssey*.

## Dubliners (1914)

*Dubliners* is a collection of **fifteen short stories** in which the failure of self-realisation of an inhabitant of Dublin is examined in biographical and psychological detail. As Joyce stated, "My intention was to write a chapter of the moral history of my country and I chose Dublin for the scene because that city seemed to me the centre of paralysis."

This **"paralysis"** — of will, courage and self-knowledge — is the central theme of the whole collection. It leads ordinary men and women to accept the limitations imposed by the social context they live in. Joyce arranged the stories to present paralysis in **four stages**: **childhood**, **youth**, **maturity** and **public life**. The final story, "The Dead," sums them all up. The narrative voice also shifts: the first three stories are in the first person, the rest in the third person.

*Dubliners* is not a fully Modernist work — it partakes of both **Realism** and **Symbolism**. It is seemingly realistic in that it perfectly recreates characters, streets, pubs and idioms of Dublin. But Joyce also uses a subtle **symbolic effect**: ordinary objects suddenly gain unexpected depth through what he called an **"epiphany"** — a moment when a simple fact or situation explodes with meaning and makes a person realise something uncomfortable about themselves. The epiphanies in *Dubliners* do not lead to great revelations; they reveal comfortable, accepted truths as fallacies.

Joyce's style is different from traditional short-story writing. Unlike Poe or Henry James, who used surprises and coincidences, Joyce followed the path of Chekhov: his stories adapt to the flow of daily life, begin in the middle of something, and stop without an apparent conclusion. Nothing remarkable happens, yet behind the routine of ordinary lives, the pressing issues of Irish life are clearly present: Ireland's relations with Britain, the tensions of family ties, and the strong presence of religion.

## Eveline

"Eveline" is the fourth story in *Dubliners* and belongs to the **youth** group. In Dublin's stagnant life, however, youth does not mean vitality: the story opens with the day dying and Eveline watching the world from her window, tired — tired not physically, but tired of the life she is living.

Eveline has agreed to run away with **Frank**, a kind and open-hearted sailor, to Buenos Aires. She reviews the familiar objects in her room, remembers the families that have gone away, thinks about her hard life: her mother is dead, her father is violent and drinks, and she has to hand over her entire wages to him. She works hard to keep the home together and take care of the younger children. Frank represents escape — he has a home waiting for her, he took her to the theatre, he sang to her about "the lass that loves a sailor."

But when she remembers the **promise she made to her dying mother** — to keep the home together as long as she could — everything changes. At the station, the boat blows its whistle, Frank holds her hand and calls "Come!" — but Eveline **cannot move**. She grips the iron railing, her face white and passive, like a helpless animal. Her eyes give Frank no sign of love or farewell or recognition. She is **paralysed**.

Joyce shows that Eveline is crushed between her desire for escape and her sense of duty. Fear of the unknown becomes paralysis — the same paralysis that runs through the entire collection.
